Part I: Notation

Parameters and statistics

  • Population parameters
    • unknown unless you have a census
    • based on population data
    • use Greek letters: \(\mu\), \(\sigma\)
  • Sample statistics
    • computed using sample data
    • use Roman letters: \(\bar X\), \(s\)
  • Observed values
    • represented by \(x\) or \(X\)
    • individuals indicated by subscripts 1, 2, …
    • \(X_1\), \(X_2\), \(X_3\),
  • Observation totals (number of observations)
    • population total = \(N\)
    • sample size = sample total = \(n\)

Summing

  • We will often need to accumulate or sum some variable of interest.

  • For example, \(A\) might denote income, with \(A_1\) being the income of the first person in our sample, \(A_2\) that for the second, and so on

  • We want to avoid writing things like: \[\begin{align*} A_1&+A_2+A_3+A_4+A_5+A_6+A_7+A_8\dots,\text{ or } \\ (A_1-B) &+ (A_2- B) + (A_3-B) + (A_4-B)\dots,\text{ or even } \\ (A_1-B)^2 &+ (A_2 -B)^2 + (A_3-B)^2 + (A_4-B)^2\dots, \end{align*}\]

  • Instead, we will use the summation operator

  • In R: use the sum() function

Summation notation

  • The sum of a variable \(X\) can be written as \[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i = X_1+X_2+\cdots+X_n\]
  • Read this as: “sum from \(i=1\) to \(n\) of \(X_i\)”
    • \(i\) indexes the observation
  • Other notation that means the same:
    • book uses \(\sum X\) and \(\sum x\)
    • we often use \(\sum_i X_i\)
  • Careful:
    • \(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2\) means “square first, then sum”
    • \(\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right)^2\) means “sum first, then square” (it includes cross-products)

Example

  • By hand
  • In R
  • Let \(X = (1,2,3)\). Then:
    • \(\sum_{i=1}^3 X_i = 1 + 2 + 3 = 6\)
    • \(\sum_{i=1}^3 X_i^2 = 1 + 4 + 9 = 14\)
    • \(\left(\sum_{i=1}^3 X_i\right)^2 = 6^2 = 36 \neq 14\)
X <- c(1, 2, 3)
c(sum_X = sum(X), sum_X_sq = sum(X^2), sum_X_all_sq = sum(X)^2)
       sum_X     sum_X_sq sum_X_all_sq 
           6           14           36
  • Types of summary measures, or summary statistics:
    • Measures of central tendency
    • locate a data set’s center
  • Measures of dispersion
    • quantify spread of data around its center
  • Measures of shape of the distribution
    • describe symmetry of a frequency/density curve
    • as well as the weight of the tails

Part II: Central Tendency

Central tendency measures

  • We will use three measures:
    • mean: average
    • median: middle value
    • mode: most frequent value

Mean

  • The sample mean (\(\bar{X}\)) of a set of \(n\) sample data: \[\bar{X} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n X_i}{n}\]
  • Balance point (see IMS dotplot)
  • Sensitive to outliers (see Bill Gates example)
  • R: mean(x)
  • The population mean (\(\mu\)) of a set of \(N\) population data: \[\mu = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N X_i}{N}\]
  • Note differences:
    • \(\mu\) vs \(\bar X\)
    • \(N\) vs \(n\)

Example: Mean

  • By hand
  • In R

  • Consider the following sample: \(X_1= 12\), \(X_2= 18\), \(X_3= 15\), \(X_4= 20\), \(X_5= 10\).

  • The mean is \[\bar X=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{5}X_i}{5}=15\]

X <- c(12,18,15,20,10)
mean(X)
[1] 15

Example: Mean

  • By hand
  • In R

Consider the following sample: \(X_1= 112\), \(X_2= 114\), \(X_3= 98\), \(X_4= 91\), \(X_5= 112\), \(X_6=64\), \(X_7=113\), \(X_8=94\), \(X_9=94\), \(X_{10}= 82\).

The mean is \[\bar X=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{10}X_i}{10}=97.4\]

X <- c(112,114,98,91,112,64,113,94,94,82)
mean(X)
[1] 97.4

Median

  • The sample median (\(\widetilde{X}\)):
    • Step 1: Order the data: \(X_{(1)} \leq X_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq X_{(n)}\)
    • \(X_{(1)},\dots,X_{(n)}\) are called the order statistics
  • If \(n\) is odd: \(\widetilde{X} = X_{\left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)}\)
  • If \(n\) is even: \(\widetilde{X} = \frac{X_{(n/2)}+X_{(n/2+1)}}{2}\)
  • In R: use sort() and median()

Example: Median

  • By hand
  • In R

  • Consider the sample (\(n=10\)): 112, 114, 98, 91, 112, 64, 113, 94, 94, 82.

  • Sorted data: \[X_{(1)}=64,\;X_{(2)}=82,\;X_{(3)}=91,\;X_{(4)}=94,\;X_{(5)}=94,\;X_{(6)}=98,\;X_{(7)}=112,\;X_{(8)}=112,\;X_{(9)}=113,\;X_{(10)}=114.\]

  • Because \(n\) is even, the median is \[\widetilde X=\frac{X_{(5)}+X_{(6)}}{2}=96.\]

  • In R, use median().
X <- c(112,114,98,91,112,64,113,94,94,82)
median(X)
[1] 96

Mode

  • The sample mode is the most frequently occurring value
  • Example: the sample \(X = (2,2,2,3,3,5)\) has mode 2
  • Example: the sample \(X = (1, 2, 3, 4, 5)\) has no mode / every value is the mode
  • Example: the sample \(X = (2, 2, 3, 3, 5, 5)\) has modes 2, 3, and 5 (multimodal distribution)
  • Example: the sample \(X = (1, 1, 1, 2, 3, 100)\) has mode 1 (mode is not affected by extreme values)

Mode: notes

  • There is always exactly one mean and exactly one median
  • There may be multiple modes (bimodal, multimodal)
  • The mode can occur away from the center
  • If every value occurs with the same frequency, the mode is not very informative
  • You can also see the mode as the peak of a density curve
  • In R: there is no built-in function for the statistical mode; use a frequency table

Part III: Dispersion

Dispersion measures

  • We will see three measures of dispersion:
  1. Variance: average squared deviation from mean
  2. Standard deviation: square root of variance
  3. Range: max minus min

Variance

  • The sample variance of a set of \(n\) sample data is the number \(s^2\) defined by \[s^2 = \frac{\sum_i (X_i - \bar{X})^2}{n-1}.\]
  • Average (squared) deviation from the mean
  • In R: use var()

There is an equivalent computational formula: \[s^2 = \frac{\sum_i X_i^2 - \frac{1}{n}(\sum_i X_i)^2}{n-1}.\] We will emphasize the definitional formula with squared deviations.

  • The population variance of a set of \(N\) population data is the number \(\sigma^2\) defined by the formula \[\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (X_i - \mu)^2}{N}.\]
  • Spot the differences:
    1. \(\mu\) instead of \(\bar X\)
    2. \(N\) instead of \(n-1\)
    3. Greek letter instead of a Roman one

Standard deviation

The standard deviation:

  • Sample standard deviation: \(s = \sqrt{s^2}\)
  • Population standard deviation: \(\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}\)
  • Interpretation:
    • same units as the observations
    • measures a typical distance from the mean
    • helps interpret frequencies later (empirical rule, Chebyshev)
  • In R: use sd() for the sample standard deviation

Example: Variance and standard deviation

  • By hand
  • In R

Let \(X = (1,2,3)\). Then \(n=3\) and \(\bar X = 2\).

  • Squared deviations: \((-1)^2,\,0^2,\,1^2\)
  • Sample variance: \[s^2 = \frac{1+0+1}{3-1} = 1\]
  • Sample standard deviation: \[s = \sqrt{s^2} = 1\]

Consider the sample: \(X_1=112\), \(X_2=114\), \(X_3=98\), \(X_4=91\), \(X_5=112\), \(X_6=64\), \(X_7=113\), \(X_8=94\), \(X_9=94\), \(X_{10}=82\).

X <- c(112, 114, 98, 91, 112, 64, 113, 94, 94, 82)
var_manual <- sum((X - mean(X))^2) / (length(X) - 1)
c(var_manual = var_manual, var = var(X), sd = sd(X))
var_manual        var         sd 
 262.48889  262.48889   16.20151

Many different shapes are consistent with the same sample mean (0 below) and standard deviation (1 below).

For a given mean and shape, the standard deviation indicates how spread out the measurements are relative to the mean.

Range

  • The range (\(R\)) is \[R=X_{(n)}-X_{(1)}\]
  • Interpretation:
    • difference between the largest and smallest observation
  • In R:
    • range() returns \((X_{(1)},X_{(n)})\) (min and max), not the scalar \(R\)
    • use min() and max() for the two endpoints

Range in R

X
 [1] 112 114  98  91 112  64 113  94  94  82
range(X)
[1]  64 114
c(min(X),max(X))
[1]  64 114

Part IV: Shape

Shape measures

  • You can think of summary measures as:
  1. I give you some sample data
  2. You compute one number that quantifies
    • where the center/location of the data is
    • how spread-out the data is
  • We can use more advanced summary statistics to quantify other features of the frequency distribution of sample data.
  • We will see two measures of shape:
    1. skewness: deviation from symmetry
    2. kurtosis: heavy-tailedness

Skewness

  • The skewness of a distribution measures its deviation from symmetry.

  • Focus on sign, not magnitude:

    • skewness = 0: symmetric distribution
    • skewness > 0: right-skewed distribution (long right tail); mean > median > mode
    • skewness < 0: left-skewed distribution (long left tail); mean < median < mode

  • In this course: focus on the sign and the picture; compute in R

Two common formulas:

  1. Pearson’s coefficient: \[\text{Skewness}=\frac{\text{mean } - \text{ mode}}{\text{Standard deviation}}\]
  2. Moment-based skewness: \[\text{Skewness} =\frac{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar X)^3}{n}}{\left(\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar X)^2}{n}\right)^{3/2}}\]

Skewness: picture

Right-skewed distribution

IMS (5.3): “When the distribution of a variable trails off to the right in this way and has a longer right tail.”

  • Quick check: For a right-skewed distribution, why do we typically see mean > median > mode?

Kurtosis

  • The kurtosis of a distribution is a measure of how heavy its tails are (how often you see extreme values).

  • The normal distribution has \(\text{kurtosis}=3\) (we will meet the normal distribution later).

  • \(\text{kurtosis} > 3\): heavier tails than normal

  • \(\text{kurtosis} < 3\): lighter tails than normal

One common formula: \[\text{Kurtosis} =\frac{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar X)^4}{n}}{\left(\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar X)^2}{n}\right)^{2}}\]

Skewness and kurtosis in R

  • To compute skewness and kurtosis for sample data in R:
    1. install once: install.packages("moments")
    2. load: library(moments)
    3. compute: skewness() and kurtosis()
  • You will practice this during Assignment 1.
library(moments)
set.seed(12345)
x <- rnorm(10000)
skewness(x)
[1] -0.02748532
kurtosis(x)
[1] 2.992872
