Part I: The MLR model

Motivation: why multiple predictors?

  • Prediction: we can often predict \(y\) more accurately if we use more information.
  • Explanation: we want to isolate the effect of one variable (\(x_1\)) by accounting for confounders (\(x_2, x_3, \dots\)).
    • “Does \(x_1\) affect \(y\), or is it just because they both move with \(x_2\)?”

The model

  • The multiple linear regression (MLR) model is: \[y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + \epsilon\]
  • Components:
    • \(\beta_0\): the intercept (predicted mean when all \(x=0\))
    • \(\beta_j\): population coefficient for predictor \(x_j\)
    • \(\epsilon\): error term (random noise)

Interpreting a partial slope

  • The coefficient \(\beta_j\) is a partial slope.
  • Interpretation: A one-unit increase in \(x_j\) is associated with a \(\beta_j\) change in the predicted mean of \(y\), holding all other predictors fixed.
  • This is the ceteris paribus (all else equal) effect.

House prices

  • Suppose we want to predict the price of a house.
  • Data on:
    • price: sale price
    • size: interior area in square feet
    • bedrooms: number of bedrooms
  • Goal: isolate the value of an extra bedroom for houses of the same size.
  • Regressing price on size and bedrooms:
    • \(\beta_{\text{size}}\): predicted price change for one more sq ft, holding the number of bedrooms constant
    • \(\beta_{\text{beds}}\): predicted price change for one more bedroom, holding square footage constant

Part II: Confounding and bias

Spurious correlations

  • Sometimes variables move together just because they share a common cause.
  • What if the relationship we observe is just picking up the effect of something else?

Omitted variable bias (OVB)

  • Omitted variable bias (OVB) is the pollution of our estimate by missing factors.
  • If we leave out a relevant variable that is correlated with our predictor, our estimate for \(\beta_1\) is biased.

Adjusted \(R^2\)

  • \(R^2\) always increases when you add a predictor, even if it’s useless.
  • Adjusted \(R^2\) penalizes the model for having too many variables: \[R_{adj}^2 = 1 - \frac{SSE / (n - k - 1)}{SS_{yy} / (n - 1)}\]
  • Use adjusted \(R^2\) to compare models with different numbers of predictors.

Exercises

Exercise: MLR interpretation

A regression model for wage is: \[\text{Wage} = 10 + 2 \times \text{Education} + 0.5 \times \text{Experience}\] Interpret the coefficient on Education.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers

What does the “2” mean?

For every additional year of education, the predicted wage increases by 2 units, holding years of experience fixed.

Exercise: Significance in MLR

In a multiple regression, \(x_1\) has a p-value of 0.45, but in a simple regression of \(y\) on \(x_1\), the p-value was 0.01.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers

How can this happen?

\(x_1\) was likely a confounder or correlated with other predictors that were actually driving \(y\). Once those predictors were included (controlled for), \(x_1\) no longer added significant information.

  • Question (4 min)
  • Answers
  1. Using the p-value approach, test whether Newspaper advertising is significantly associated with Sales at the 1% level. Report the p-value.
  2. What percentage of the variation in Sales is explained by this regression model?
  1. \(H_0: \beta_{\text{news}} = 0\) vs \(H_a: \beta_{\text{news}} \neq 0\). The \(p\)-value for Newspaper is \(0.954\). Since \(0.954 > 0.01\), we do not reject \(H_0\). There is no significant association once we control for TV and Radio.
  2. The Multiple R-squared is \(0.9026\), so about 90.3% of the variation in Sales is explained by the model.
