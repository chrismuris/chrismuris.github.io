Part I: Review and interpretation

Motivation: why regression?

  • Previously, we used descriptive statistics (mean, SD) to summarize a single variable.

  • Regression lets us study the association between two or more variables.

  • Goals:

    • explanation: how does \(x\) relate to \(y\)?
    • prediction: what is the likely value of \(y\) given \(x\)?

Simple linear regression model

  • The simple linear regression model is \(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \epsilon\)
  • The deterministic part is \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x\), which is the population line
    • gives the mean of \(y\) for any given \(x\)
  • The random part is \(\epsilon\), the error term
    • has mean 0 and standard deviation \(\sigma\)
  • We estimate this using a sample of size \(n\):
    • \(\widehat y = \widehat\beta_0 + \widehat\beta_1 x\)

Interpretation: slope and intercept

  • The slope (\(\widehat\beta_1\)) is the estimated difference in the predicted mean of \(y\) for a one-unit increase in \(x\).
    • always include units in your interpretation!
  • The intercept (\(\widehat\beta_0\)) is the predicted mean of \(y\) if \(x=0\).
    • only interpret the intercept if \(x=0\) is plausible and within the range of the data

When \(x\) is categorical (0/1)

  • We use indicator variables for categories (also called dummy variables).
  • Coding:
    • \(x=0\): baseline group
    • \(x=1\): comparison group
  • Meaning of the coefficients:
    • \(\widehat\beta_0\): predicted mean of \(y\) for the baseline group
    • \(\widehat\beta_1\): the difference in predicted means (comparison \(\dots\) baseline)

Part II: Goodness of fit (\(R^2\))

The advertising data

Advertising budgets

  • Data on sales of a product in 200 different markets.
  • Outcome (\(y\)): Sales (in thousands of units).
  • Predictor (\(x\)): TV (advertising budget in thousands of dollars).
  • Goal: quantify how much of the sales variation is explained by TV ads.

Comparing prediction rules

  • How much does knowing \(x\) help us predict \(y\)?
  • Baseline rule: use the sample mean \(\bar y\).
    • error for each point is \((y_i - \bar y)\)
  • Regression rule: use the fitted line \(\widehat y_i\).
    • error for each point is \((y_i - \widehat y_i)\)
  • Goal: quantify the reduction in error.

Visual intuition: two approximating lines

  • Left panel uses \(\bar y\) (total variation).

  • Right panel uses the regression line (unexplained variation).

  • \(R^2\) measures the reduction in error.

Total vs unexplained variation

  • The total variation (\(SS_{yy}\)) is \(\sum (y_i - \bar y)^2\)
    • called “total sum of squares”
    • error when using only the mean
  • The unexplained variation (\(SSE\)) is \(\sum (y_i - \widehat y_i)^2\)
    • called “sum of squared errors”
    • error remaining after fitting the line

Coefficient of determination (\(R^2\))

  • The coefficient of determination (\(R^2\)) describes how well the line fits the data

  • It is the fraction of the sample variation in \(y\) explained by the linear relationship with \(x\)

  • Range: \(0 \le R^2 \le 1\)

  • Equivalent formulas (SZ 10.6):

    1. \(R^2 = 1 - \frac{SSE}{SS_{yy}}\)
    2. \(R^2 = \frac{SS_{xy}^2}{SS_{xx}SS_{yy}} = r^2\)
    3. \(R^2 = \widehat{\beta}_1 \frac{SS_{xy}}{SS_{yy}}\)

Part III: Inference for the slope

The hypothesis test

  • The estimated slope \(\widehat\beta_1\) is a statistic; it varies from sample to sample

  • We want to know if the population slope \(\beta_1\) is zero (no association)

  • \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) (no linear association)

  • \(H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0\) (two-sided association)

  • Test Statistic: \[T = \frac{\widehat \beta_1 - 0}{SE(\widehat{\beta}_1)}\]

  • Distribution: \(t\) with \(df = n-2\)

Standard error of the slope

  • The standard error of the slope measures the precision of our estimate: \[SE(\widehat{\beta}_1) = \frac{s_\epsilon}{\sqrt{SS_{xx}}}\]

  • Precision improves (smaller \(SE\)) if:

    • the error standard deviation (\(s_\epsilon\)) is small (low noise)
    • the variation in \(x\) (\(SS_{xx}\)) is large (wider range)

Confidence interval for the slope

  • We can also estimate the range of plausible values for the population slope \(\beta_1\)
  • Formula: \[\widehat{\beta}_1 \pm t^* \cdot SE(\widehat{\beta}_1)\]
    • where \(t^*\) is the critical value for \(df = n-2\)
  • Conditions for inference:
    • linearity: the relationship should be approximately linear
    • constant variance: the spread of residuals should be similar across \(x\)
    • independence: observations should not be related to each other
    • normality: residuals should be approximately normal

Exercise: Significance from a regression table

A regression of test score on hours of study gives \(\widehat\beta_1 = 2.8\) with \(SE(\widehat\beta_1) = 1.5\) and \(df = 18\).

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. Compute the \(t\)-statistic.
  2. The critical value for \(df = 18\) at the 5% level (two-sided) is \(t^* = 2.101\). Is the association significant?
  1. \(T = 2.8 / 1.5 \approx 1.87\)
  2. Since \(|T| = 1.87 < 2.101\), we fail to reject \(H_0\). The association is not significant at the 5% level.

Part IV: Worked example

Silicofluoride and lead

Shafer and Zhang, 10.8, Exercise 1

The data give the amount \(x\) of silicofluoride in water (mg/L) and lead \(y\) in blood (μg/dL) for \(n=10\) children.

The Task:

  1. Calculate the least squares regression line.
  2. Compute and interpret the coefficient of determination (\(R^2\)).
  3. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the population slope \(\beta_1\).
  4. Test \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) vs. \(H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0\) at the 5% level.

Raw Data:

\(x\) 0.0 0.0 1.1 1.4 1.6 1.7 2.0 2.0 2.2 2.2
\(y\) 0.3 0.1 4.7 3.2 5.1 7.0 5.0 6.1 8.6 9.5

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 1: Compute preliminary sums

Using \(n = 10\):

  • \(\sum x = 0.0 + 0.0 + \dots + 2.2 = 14.2\)
  • \(\sum y = 0.3 + 0.1 + \dots + 9.5 = 49.6\)
  • \(\sum x^2 = 0.0^2 + 0.0^2 + \dots + 2.2^2 = 26.3\)
  • \(\sum y^2 = 0.3^2 + 0.1^2 + \dots + 9.5^2 = 333.86\)
  • \(\sum xy = (0.0 \times 0.3) + \dots + (2.2 \times 9.5) = 91.73\)

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 2: Compute sample means and sums of squares

  • \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} = \frac{14.2}{10} = 1.42\)
  • \(\bar{y} = \frac{\sum y}{n} = \frac{49.6}{10} = 4.96\)

Sums of squares:

  • \(SS_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \frac{1}{n} (\sum x)^2 = 26.3 - \frac{14.2^2}{10} = 6.136\)
  • \(SS_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \frac{1}{n} (\sum y)^2 = 333.86 - \frac{49.6^2}{10} = 87.844\)
  • \(SS_{xy} = \sum xy - \frac{1}{n} (\sum x)(\sum y) = 91.73 - \frac{14.2 \times 49.6}{10} = 21.298\)

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 3: Calculate the regression slope

  • The formula for the slope is: \[\widehat\beta_1 = \frac{SS_{xy}}{SS_{xx}}\]
  • Plugging in our values: \[\widehat\beta_1 = \frac{21.298}{6.136} \approx 3.47\]
  • Interpretation: For every 1 mg/L increase in silicofluoride, the predicted mean lead in blood increases by 3.47 μg/dL.

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 4: Calculate the regression intercept

  • The formula for the intercept is: \[\widehat\beta_0 = \bar{y} - \widehat\beta_1 \bar{x}\]
  • Plugging in our values: \[\widehat\beta_0 = 4.96 - (3.47 \times 1.42)\] \[\widehat\beta_0 = 4.96 - 4.9274 \approx 0.03\]
  • Our fitted regression line is: \[\widehat{y} = 0.03 + 3.47x\]

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 5: Assess the goodness of fit (\(R^2\))

  • We can use the formula based on sums of squares: \[R^2 = \frac{SS_{xy}^2}{SS_{xx} SS_{yy}}\]
  • Plugging in our values: \[R^2 = \frac{21.298^2}{6.136 \times 87.844} = \frac{453.605}{539.011} \approx 0.84\]
  • Interpretation: 84% of the variation in children’s blood lead levels is explained by the linear relationship with silicofluoride levels in their water.

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 6: Compute the standard error of the slope

  • First, we need \(SSE\) (using the formula \(SSE = SS_{yy} - \widehat\beta_1 SS_{xy}\)): \[SSE = 87.844 - 3.47 \times 21.298 = 87.844 - 73.904 \approx 13.94\]
  • Then, the sample variance of the residuals (\(s_\epsilon^2\)) with \(df = n - 2 = 8\): \[s_\epsilon^2 = \frac{SSE}{n - 2} = \frac{13.94}{8} \approx 1.743\]
  • The standard error of the slope is: \[SE(\widehat{\beta}_1) = \frac{s_\epsilon}{\sqrt{SS_{xx}}} = \frac{\sqrt{1.743}}{\sqrt{6.136}} = \frac{1.320}{2.477} \approx 0.53\]

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 7: Construct a 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_1\)

  • The critical value for \(df = 8\) at 95% confidence is \(t^* = 2.306\).
  • The confidence interval is: \[\widehat\beta_1 \pm t^* \cdot SE(\widehat\beta_1) = 3.47 \pm 2.306 \times 0.53 = 3.47 \pm 1.22\]
  • The 95% CI is \((2.25, 4.69)\).
  • Interpretation: We are 95% confident that each additional 1 mg/L of silicofluoride in water is associated with between 2.25 and 4.69 μg/dL more lead in blood.

Silicofluoride and lead (continued)

Step 8: Calculate test statistic and conclude

  • Hypotheses: \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) versus \(H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0\)
  • The test statistic is: \[T = \frac{\widehat\beta_1 - 0}{SE(\widehat{\beta}_1)} = \frac{3.47}{0.53} \approx 6.55\]
  • With \(df = 8\), a \(T\)-statistic of \(6.55\) is very large (\(p < 0.001\)).
  • Conclusion: We reject \(H_0\). The data provide strong evidence of a positive linear association between silicofluoride in water and lead in blood.
