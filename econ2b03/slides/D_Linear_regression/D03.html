Part I: Review and interpretation

Motivation: why regression?

  • Previously, we used descriptive statistics (mean, SD) to summarize a single variable.
  • Regression lets us study the association between two or more variables.
  • Goals:
    • Explanation: how does \(x\) relate to \(y\)?
    • Prediction: what is the likely value of \(y\) given \(x\)?

Simple linear regression model

  • Model: \(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \epsilon\)
  • Deterministic part: \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x\) (population line)
  • Random part: \(\epsilon\) (error term, mean 0, SD \(\sigma\))
  • We estimate this using a sample of size \(n\):
    • \(\widehat y = \widehat\beta_0 + \widehat\beta_1 x\)

Interpretation: slope and intercept

  • Slope (\(\widehat\beta_1\)): the estimated difference in the predicted mean of \(y\) for a one-unit increase in \(x\).
  • Intercept (\(\widehat\beta_0\)): the predicted mean of \(y\) if \(x=0\).
  • Caution: only interpret the intercept if \(x=0\) is plausible and within the range of the data.

Example: gift aid and family income

  • For a 1-unit increase in family_income, predicted gift_aid changes by \(\widehat\beta_1\) units.
  • Always check the units! (e.g., $1,000s of dollars).
coef(elm_lm)
  (Intercept) family_income 
  24.31932901   -0.04307165
  • Interpretation: about $43 less aid per $1,000 increase in family income.

When \(x\) is categorical (0/1)

  • \(x=0\): baseline group
  • \(x=1\): comparison group
  • Interpretation:
    • \(\widehat\beta_0\): predicted mean of \(y\) for the baseline group.
    • \(\widehat\beta_1\): the difference in predicted means (comparison − baseline).

Example: used vs new video games

  • Baseline group is new.
  • Predicted mean price for a used game: \(\widehat\beta_0 + \widehat\beta_1(1)\).
coef(mk_lm)
(Intercept)    condused 
   53.77068   -10.89958
  • Used games sell for about $10.90 less (on average) than new games in this sample.

Part II: Goodness of fit (\(R^2\))

Comparing prediction rules

  • Baseline predictor: \(\widehat y = \bar y\) (ignore \(x\))
  • Regression predictor: \(\widehat y = \widehat\beta_0 + \widehat\beta_1 x\) (use \(x\))
  • Question: how much better do we do by using \(x\)?

Visual intuition: two approximating lines

  • Left panel uses \(\bar y\) (total variation).
  • Right panel uses the regression line (unexplained variation).
  • \(R^2\) measures the reduction in error.

Total vs unexplained variation

  • Total variation (\(SS_{yy}\)): \(\sum (y_i - \bar y)^2\)
    • error when using only the mean
  • Unexplained variation (\(SSE\)): \(\sum (y_i - \widehat y_i)^2\)
    • error remaining after fitting the line

Coefficient of determination (\(R^2\))

  • \(R^2\) describes how well the line fits the data.
  • Formula: \[R^2 = 1 - \frac{SSE}{SS_{yy}}\]
  • Interpretation: the fraction of the sample variation in \(y\) “explained” by the linear relationship with \(x\).
  • Range: \(0 \le R^2 \le 1\).

Multiple formulas for \(R^2\)

For simple linear regression, we can compute \(R^2\) in three equivalent ways (SZ 10.6):

  1. \[R^2 = 1 - \frac{SSE}{SS_{yy}}\]
  2. \[R^2 = \frac{SS_{xy}^2}{SS_{xx}SS_{yy}} = r^2\]
  3. \[R^2 = \widehat{\beta}_1 \frac{SS_{xy}}{SS_{yy}}\]
  • Use whichever is easiest given your previous calculations!

Interpreting \(R^2\) in R

  • In summary(lm(...)), this is labeled Multiple R-squared.
summary(adv_lm_tv)$r.squared
[1] 0.8121757
  • Interpretation: about 81% of the variation in Sales is explained by TV advertising spending in this model.

Part III: Inference for the slope

Why inference?

  • The estimated slope \(\widehat\beta_1\) is a statistic; it varies from sample to sample.
  • We want to know if the population slope \(\beta_1\) is zero (no association).

The hypothesis test

  • \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) (no linear association)
  • \(H_a: \beta_1 \neq 0\) (two-sided association)
  • Test Statistic: \[T = \frac{\widehat \beta_1 - 0}{SE(\widehat{\beta}_1)}\]
  • Distribution: \(t\) with \(df = n-2\).

Standard error of the slope

  • The standard error of the slope measures the precision of our estimate: \[SE(\widehat{\beta}_1) = \frac{s_\epsilon}{\sqrt{SS_{xx}}}\]
  • Precision improves (smaller \(SE\)) if:
    • the error SD (\(s_\epsilon\)) is small (low noise)
    • the variation in \(x\) (\(SS_{xx}\)) is large (wider range)

Conditions for inference

  • Linearity: the relationship should be approximately linear.
  • Constant variance: the spread of residuals should be similar across \(x\).
  • Independence: observations should not be related to each other.
  • Normality: residuals should be approximately normal (needed for small \(n\)).

Reading inferential output

  • The third and fourth columns in the Coefficients table give the \(t\)-statistic and the \(p\)-value.
summary(adv_lm_tv)$coefficients
              Estimate  Std. Error  t value     Pr(>|t|)
(Intercept) 6.97482149 0.322553485 21.62377 5.027719e-54
TV          0.05546477 0.001895551 29.26050 7.927912e-74
  • For TV, \(p < 0.05\), so we reject \(H_0\). There is strong evidence of an association.

Part IV: Worked example

Silicofluoride and lead example

Shafer and Zhang, 10.8, Exercise 1

The data give the amount \(x\) of silicofluoride in water (mg/L) and lead \(y\) in blood (μg/dL) for ten children.

  • Step 1: compute \(SS_{xx}, SS_{xy}, SS_{yy}\).
  • Step 2: find \(\widehat\beta_1 = SS_{xy}/SS_{xx}\) and \(\widehat\beta_0\).
  • Step 3: compute \(R^2\) and \(SSE\).
  • Step 4: test \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\) vs \(H_a: \beta_1 > 0\) at 5%.

Calculations recap

  • \(\widehat\beta_1 \approx 3.47\)
  • \(R^2 \approx 0.84\)
  • \(T \approx 6.52\) (\(df=8\))
  • \(p \text{-value} \approx 0.0001\)
  • Conclusion: reject \(H_0\). The data provide sufficient evidence of a positive association between silicofluoride in water and lead in blood.

Exercises

Exercise: interpret \(R^2\)

In a regression of wage on years of education, \(R^2 = 0.25\). Interpret this value.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers

What does 0.25 tell us about the relationship?

About 25% of the variation in wages in the sample is explained by the linear relationship with education.

Exercise: significance

A regression reports a slope of 5.2 with a p-value of 0.08.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers

Is the association significant at the 5% level?

No, because \(0.08 > 0.05\), we fail to reject the null hypothesis of no association.
