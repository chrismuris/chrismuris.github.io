Regression in economics

  • Regression is the workhorse tool for empirical economics.
  • We start with simple linear regression:
    • one outcome variable \(y\)
    • one predictor variable \(x\)
  • Think of it as a way to summarize the association between two variables and make predictions.

Part I: Introduction to Regression

Motivation: why regression?

  • Economics is full of questions about relationships between variables.

  • Simple linear regression gives us a summary line and a prediction rule for those relationships.

  • We will look at two examples:

    1. advertising expenditure and sales
    2. class-size and student performance

Part II: Linear Relationships

Deterministic vs random relationships

  • A relationship between \(x\) and \(y\) is deterministic if knowing \(x\) completely determines \(y\).
  • In most economic data, relationships involve randomness:
    • the same \(x\) value can correspond to different \(y\) values . . .
  • Regression lines are average responses because each value of \(x\) accompanies a cloud of \(y\) outcomes.

Linear relationships: slope and intercept

  • A relationship is linear if points lie on a straight line.
  • Line equation: \[y = a x + b\]
  • Interpretation:
    • slope \(a\): change in \(y\) for a 1-unit increase in \(x\)
    • intercept \(b\): value of \(y\) when \(x = 0\)

Scatter plots

  • A scatter plot displays pairs \((x,y)\):
    • \(x\) on the horizontal axis
    • \(y\) on the vertical axis
  • First things to look for:
    • direction (upward/downward)
    • strength (tight/loose cloud)
    • form (linear/nonlinear)

Part III: Correlation

Correlation: what it measures

  • The correlation coefficient summarizes the strength of a linear relationship.
  • Intuition: how close are the points to a straight line?
  • Correlation is unitless and ranges from -1 to 1.

Correlation: examples

  • Plot A (bananas vs potatoes): -0.54
  • Plot B (cassava vs soybeans): 0.16
  • Plot C (cassava vs maize): 0.46
  • Plot D (cocoa vs bananas): -0.44
  • Plot E (peas vs barley): 0.69
  • Plot F (wheat vs barley): 0.85

Quick check

Which plot looks closest to zero correlation? Which looks strongest?

Correlation coefficient

  • For a sample of \(n\) pairs \((x,y)\), the correlation is: \[r = \frac{SS_{xy}}{\sqrt{SS_{xx}SS_{yy}}}\]
  • Where:
    • \(SS_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \frac{1}{n}(\sum x)^2\)
    • \(SS_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \frac{1}{n}(\sum y)^2\)
    • \(SS_{xy} = \sum xy - \frac{1}{n}(\sum x)(\sum y)\)

Interpreting correlation

  • Range: \(-1 \le r \le 1\)
  • Sign:
    • \(r < 0\): as \(x\) increases, \(y\) tends to decrease
    • \(r > 0\): as \(x\) increases, \(y\) tends to increase
  • Magnitude:
    • \(|r|\) close to 1: strong linear relationship
    • \(|r|\) close to 0: weak linear relationship
  • What \(r\) does not tell you:
    • slope of the relationship
    • nonlinear association
    • causality
