Part I: CI for means when \(\sigma\) is unknown

What changes from previous lecture?

  • Previously, we assumed the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) was known.
  • In practice, that is unusual.
  • So the practical question is:

If \(\sigma\) is unknown, how do we estimate the standard error of \(\bar X\)?

  • Answer: we replace \(\sigma\) with the sample standard deviation \(s\).

Critical values refresher

We will need critical values that match the confidence level:

Three values worth remembering

  • 90% CI: \(z_{\alpha/2} = z_{0.05} \approx 1.645\)
  • 95% CI: \(z_{\alpha/2} = z_{0.025} \approx 1.96\)
  • 99% CI: \(z_{\alpha/2} = z_{0.005} \approx 2.576\)

CI for mu when \(\sigma\) is unknown

For \(\sigma\) unknown and \(n\) large, use \(s\) instead of \(\sigma\):

Large-sample CI for a population mean (\(\sigma\) unknown)

The large-sample \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval for a population mean if \(\sigma\) is unknown is \[\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right),\] where a sample is considered large when \(n \geq 30\).

Same logic as previous lecture

  • Previously: \[\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2}\left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]
  • Now: \[\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2}\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]
  • So the main new idea is not a new philosophy.
  • The main new idea is: estimate the unknown population variability from the sample.

Groceries

A random sample of 49 students finds:

  • sample mean weekly grocery spending: 35 CAD
  • sample standard deviation: 14 CAD

Construct a 99% confidence interval for the population mean weekly grocery spending. Then interpret it.

Worked solution

  • Ingredients:
    • \(n = 49\), so the large-sample CI is justified
    • \(\bar X = 35\)
    • \(s = 14\)
    • for a 99% CI, \(z_{0.005} = 2.576\)
  • Estimated standard error: \[\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}=\frac{14}{\sqrt{49}}=2\]
  • Margin of error: \[2.576 \times 2 = 5.152\]
35 + 2.576 * 2 * c(-1, 1)
[1] 29.848 40.152
  • So the 99% CI is approximately \([29.85, 40.15]\).
  • Interpretation:
    • We are 99% confident that the population mean weekly grocery spending lies between about 29.85 CAD and 40.15 CAD, in the repeated-sampling sense.

Repeated-sampling reminder

  • The interval is random because it depends on the sample.
  • The population mean is fixed.
  • Confidence refers to the long-run success rate of the method…
  • … not to the probability that this one fixed interval contains \(\mu\).

  • Answers

Without changing the sample, compare the 90% and 99% confidence intervals.

Which one is wider, and why?

The 99% CI is wider because it uses a larger critical value.

  • 90% CI uses \(z_{0.05} = 1.645\)
  • 99% CI uses \(z_{0.005} = 2.576\)
  • The sample and standard error are unchanged
  • Higher confidence means a larger margin of error

Part II: CI for proportions

Why proportions are special

  • Many empirical questions are yes/no questions:
    • employed or not
    • voted or not
    • received a callback or not
  • In that setting, the parameter of interest is a population proportion \(p\).
  • The sample analogue is the sample proportion \(\widehat P\).

CI for p

Large-sample CIs for \(p\) are similar to those for \(\mu\):

Large-sample CI for a population proportion

The large-sample \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval for a population proportion is \[\widehat P \pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sqrt{\widehat P(1-\widehat P)}}{\sqrt n}.\]

  • Use this when the sample is large enough that
    • \(n\widehat P \geq 5\)
    • \(n(1-\widehat P) \geq 5\)
  • In SZ, the condition is phrased more formally. In class, we will use this simpler rule of thumb.

Same logic, different standard error

  • The structure is still:
    • point estimate \(\pm\) critical value times standard error
  • What changes is the standard error: \[\text{SE}(\widehat P)\approx \sqrt{\frac{\widehat P(1-\widehat P)}{n}}\]

Payday loan regulations

Suppose a random sample of 826 payday loan borrowers is surveyed about proposed new regulations for payday lenders.

In the sample, 70% support the new regulations.

Construct a 95% confidence interval for \(p\), the population proportion of payday borrowers who support these regulations.

Worked solution

  • Check the conditions:
    • \(\widehat P = 0.70\)
    • \(n\widehat P = 826 \times 0.70 \approx 578 \geq 5\)
    • \(n(1-\widehat P) = 826 \times 0.30 \approx 248 \geq 5\)
  • So the large-sample CI is appropriate.
p_hat <- 0.70
n <- 826
sqrt(p_hat * (1 - p_hat) / n)
[1] 0.01594482
p_hat + 1.96 * sqrt(p_hat * (1 - p_hat) / n) * c(-1, 1)
[1] 0.6687482 0.7312518
  • So the 95% CI is approximately \([0.669, 0.731]\).
  • Interpretation:
    • We are 95% confident that the true support rate lies between 66.9% and 73.1%, in the repeated-sampling sense.

Callback rate

Suppose a career centre tracks a random sample of 900 job applications submitted by recent graduates.

Out of those 900 applications, 300 receive a callback.

Construct a 95% confidence interval for \(p\), the population callback rate.

Backup example: callback rate solution

  • \(\widehat P = 300/900 = 1/3\)
  • The large-sample conditions hold easily.
p_hat <- 300 / 900
p_hat + 1.96 * sqrt(p_hat * (1 - p_hat) / 900) * c(-1, 1)
[1] 0.3025349 0.3641318
  • So the 95% CI is approximately \([0.30, 0.36]\).

Interpreting the callback example

  • Our best estimate of the callback rate is 33.3%.
  • A 95% confidence interval for the population callback rate is about 30% to 36%.
  • The interval is not centered at 0 or 1 because the observed callback rate is not near those extremes.
  • How to interpret \([0.3, 0.36]\)?
    • We are 95% confident that the true callback rate is between 30% and 36%, in the sense that 95% of intervals constructed this way under repeated random sampling would contain the true rate.

Exercise: six-year graduation rate

Exercise (adapted from SZ 7.3)

A university examines 600 randomly selected entering students and finds that 312 graduated within six years.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. Compute the sample proportion.
  2. Construct a 95% CI for the six-year graduation rate.
  • \(\widehat P = 312/600 = 0.52\)
  • Since \(600(0.52)\) and \(600(0.48)\) are both much larger than 5, the large-sample CI applies.
  • The interval is \[ 0.52 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{0.52(0.48)}{600}} \approx (0.480,\ 0.560) \]

Verify the large-sample conditions, then compare the 90% and 99% CIs for \(p\) in each case.

  • Question (4 min)
  • Answers
  1. \(n = 80\), \(\widehat P = 0.4\)
  2. \(n = 325\), \(\widehat P = 0.4\)

Both satisfy the large-sample conditions.

  • For \(n=80\), the standard error is about \(0.0548\):
    • 90% CI: \(0.4 \pm 1.645 \times 0.0548 \approx (0.310,\ 0.490)\)
    • 99% CI: \(0.4 \pm 2.576 \times 0.0548 \approx (0.259,\ 0.541)\)
  • For \(n=325\), the standard error is about \(0.0272\):
    • 90% CI: \(0.4 \pm 1.645 \times 0.0272 \approx (0.355,\ 0.445)\)
    • 99% CI: \(0.4 \pm 2.576 \times 0.0272 \approx (0.330,\ 0.470)\)
  • In both cases, the 99% CI is wider, and the larger sample gives the narrower interval.

Takeaway from Parts I and II

  • For means and proportions, confidence intervals have the same basic architecture.
  • Precision comes from:
    • lower variability
    • larger sample size
    • lower confidence level

Part III: Minimum sample size

Turning precision into a design question

So far, we have asked:

  • given a sample, how wide is the confidence interval?

Now we ask the reverse question:

  • if I want a narrow interval, how large should the sample be?

Minimum sample size for a mean

Minimum sample size for estimating a population mean

To estimate a population mean to within \(E\) units at \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence, use

\[n=\frac{(z_{\alpha/2})^2 \sigma^2}{E^2},\]

rounded up to the next integer.

Where the formula comes from

  • Start from the margin of error formula \[E = z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\]
  • Solve for \(n\).
  • This is the same logic as before.
  • We are just choosing \(n\) to hit a target precision level.

Minimum sample size for a proportion

Minimum sample size for estimating a population proportion

To estimate a population proportion to within \(E\) units at \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence, use

\[n=\frac{(z_{\alpha/2})^2 \widehat p (1-\widehat p)}{E^2},\]

rounded up to the next integer.

Choosing \(\widehat p\)

  • Sometimes you have a prior estimate from older data.
  • Sometimes you make an informed guess.
  • If you want the most conservative sample size, use \(\widehat p = 0.5\).
  • Why? Because \(\widehat p(1-\widehat p)\) is largest at \(0.5\).

Example: student fees and a football stadium

Exercise (adapted from OpenIntro)

A university newspaper wants to estimate the proportion of students who support a $200 annual fee increase to fund a new football stadium.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers

How large should the sample be to get a 95% CI with margin of error at most 0.04, if no prior estimate of support is available?

  • Use the conservative choice \(\widehat p = 0.50\).
  • Then \[ n=\frac{1.96^2(0.5)(0.5)}{0.04^2}\approx 600.25 \]
  • Round up: \(n = 601\).

Translating the stadium-fee problem

  • Confidence level 95% means \(z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96\).
  • Target margin of error: \(E = 0.04\).
  • No prior estimate is available, so use the conservative choice \(\widehat p = 0.50\).

Computing the required sample size

1.96^2 * 0.5 * 0.5 / (0.04)^2
[1] 600.25
  • Round up: \(n = 601\).
  • This is the minimum sample size that guarantees the target margin of error under the conservative assumption.
