Overview

  • Why this matters: confidence intervals quantify uncertainty around population parameters.
  • Instead of a single guess for a population parameter, we give a range that is likely to contain the true value.
  • What you will learn:

    2. how to construct a CI for \(\mu\) when \(\sigma\) is known

Important thing to keep in mind for today:

  1. just like the sample statistic \(\bar X\) …
  2. … the CI \([\bar X - E, \bar X + E]\) is random:
    • it takes a different value for each sample

  3. Population parameter \(\mu\) is not random

Part I: Point estimation

Point estimation

  • Point estimation is the estimation of a population parameter by a single sample statistic

  • When doing point estimation, the sample statistic is called a point estimate of the population parameter

  • Point estimates tell us nothing about how reliable or precise the estimate is

Example: Coin flipping

I am trying to determine whether a coin is fair.

  • I do a few trials, flipping the coin a number of times
  • I find that, exactly 50% of the time, the coin comes up heads
  • Is this informative?

Example: Coin flipping (continued)

How informative “50%” is depends on how many trials we did.

  • Scenario 1: I flipped the coin twice, and it came up heads once
  • Scenario 2: I flipped the coin 2,000 times, and it came up heads 1,000 times
  • In both cases, the point estimates are identical
  • However, case 2 makes us much more confident that the coin is fair
  • In general, the degree of confidence will depend on:
    • sample size
    • population standard deviation (unknown)
    • the sampling distribution of the sample statistic
  • Confidence intervals take this into account, and answer the question:
    • can a sample statistic be expected to be close to the unknown population parameter?

Part II: Interval estimation

Interval estimation

  • Interval estimation is the estimation of a population parameter using an interval
  • The margin of error, \(E\), is the number added to and subtracted from the point estimate to produce the interval estimate
  • A 95% confidence interval for \(\mu\) is an interval with endpoints \(\bar X \pm E\), computed from the sample data in such a way that 95% of all intervals constructed by this process will contain the parameter of interest
    • it is often written as the interval \([\bar X - E, \bar X + E]\)

  • Confidence intervals do not have to be 95% CIs
  • The level of confidence is the proportion of confidence intervals which, if constructed under repeated random sampling, would contain the parameter of interest

CI for \(\mu\) when \(\sigma\) is known

  • The large sample \(100(1-\alpha)\)% confidence interval for a population mean if \(\sigma\) is known is \[\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]
    • in this course, a sample is considered large when \(n \geq 30\)
  • The margin of error is \[E = z_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]
  • A different way to write this interval is \(\left[\bar X - z_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right),\bar X + z_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]\)

Derivation of CI formula

  • To understand where this formula comes from, we use the Central Limit Theorem (CLT)
    • the CLT gives us the sampling distribution of \(\bar X\)
    • we use the CLT to construct the CI
  • The CLT approximates the sampling distribution
  • Sampling distribution is the distribution of a sample statistic (e.g., \(\bar X\)) across all possible samples of a given size from the population
  • It tells us “how far away” the point estimate is from the population parameter

Sampling distribution

Sampling distribution of \(\bar X\) when \(\mu_{\bar X}=2\) and \(\sigma^2_{\bar X}=9\).

  • By the CLT, approximately \(\bar X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)\)
  • The variance is \(\sigma^2/n\)
    • we can make the variance of \(\bar X\) smaller by increasing \(n\)
    • we do not control \(\sigma^2\)
  • The error of estimation is \[\vert \bar X - \mu,\vert\]
    • absolute difference between point estimate and parameter
  • Because \(\bar X\) is approximately normal, about 95% of all observations lie within 1.96 standard deviations of the mean: \[\begin{align*} 0.95 &= P(|Z|<1.96) \\ &= P(|(\bar X - \mu )/\sigma_{\bar X}|<1.96) \\ &= P(|\bar X-\mu|<1.96\sigma_{\bar X}) \\ &=0.95. \end{align*}\]
  • We can use \(E = 1.96\sigma_{\bar X} = 1.96 \sigma / \sqrt{n}\) for a 95% CI for \(\mu\)
    • Note: the empirical rule suggests 2, but 1.96 is more precise
    • Could use tables for an even more precise value, but 1.96 is good enough for our purposes

  • Rearrange that derivation to obtain the CI: \[\begin{align*} 0.95 &= P(|\bar X-\mu|<1.96\sigma_{\bar X}) \\ &= P(-1.96\sigma_{\bar X} < \bar X-\mu <1.96\sigma_{\bar X}) \\ &= P(\bar X-1.96\sigma_{\bar X} < \mu < \bar X + 1.96\sigma_{\bar X}). \end{align*}\]

  • So, a \(95\%\) CI for \(\mu\) is given by \[ \left[\bar X-1.96\sigma_{\bar X},\bar X + 1.96\sigma_{\bar X}\right] = \left[\bar X-1.96 \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},\bar X + 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right] \]

  • The key mathematical expression is \[\begin{align*} 0.95 &= P\left(\bar X-1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} < \mu < \bar X + 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \right) \\ &= P\left(\bar X - E < \mu < \bar X + E \right) \end{align*}\]

  • Consider the following questions:

    • what does the \(P\) refer to exactly?
    • what happens to the interval as \(n\) grows?
    • what happens as \(\sigma\) grows, and what does that mean?

The level of confidence

  • We have focused on a level of confidence of 95%, for which \(E = 1.96 \sigma_{\bar X}\)

  • The researcher chooses the level of confidence:

    • the 90% CI analogous to the one above is \(\left[\bar X-1.645\sigma_{\bar X},\bar X+1.645\sigma_{\bar X}\right]\)
    • the 99% CI analogous to the one above is \(\left[\bar X-2.576\sigma_{\bar X},\bar X+2.576\sigma_{\bar X}\right]\)

  • There is a trade-off between the width of the CI and the level of confidence:

    • which of the two CIs above is more likely to contain \(\mu\)?
    • is the 90% CI wider or narrower than the 95% one?

Exercise: Small business employees

A survey of a random sample of \(n=49\) Canadian small businesses revealed that the average number of employees was 15.54. The variance of the number of employees is known from past data to be \(\sigma^2=9\).

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Construct a 90% CI for the population mean number of employees \(\mu\)
  • The formula is \(\bar X\pm 1.645\sigma_{\bar X}\)
  • We calculate \(\sigma_{\bar X}=\sqrt{\sigma^2/n}=\sqrt{9/49} = 3/7 \approx 0.429\)
  • The 90% CI is \(15.54\pm 1.645\times 0.429=[14.83,16.25]\)

Part III: Interpretation of confidence intervals

Interpretation of confidence intervals

  • We correctly say that:
    • “the process of constructing a 95% CI will contain the true population parameter 95% of the time”
  • We often say, imprecisely (but acceptable in conversation, not on exams):
    • “we are 95% confident that the interval contains \(\mu\)”
  • We cannot say:
    • “there is a 95% probability that the interval contains \(\mu\)”
    • because for a given sample, the interval either does or does not contain \(\mu\) (probability 1 or 0)
  • If we performed the following 3-step experiment many times, independently:
    1. draw a random sample of size \(n\) from the population
    2. compute \(\bar X\)
    3. construct the CI
  • Then 95% of the resulting CIs will contain \(\mu\)

Section 7.2: Small sample estimation

  • Self-study the concepts in this section (SZ 7.2)
  • We may cover exercises from it in a future lecture
  • Definitions follow for completeness

  • Student’s \(t\)-distribution is a distribution of a continuous random variable that resembles the standard normal distribution but has heavier tails

  • The degrees of freedom is a number that specifies a particular \(t\)-distribution and that is computed based on the sample size

  • The small sample \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval for a population mean for \(\sigma\) known is \(\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\)

    • the population must be normally distributed, and a sample is considered small when \(n<30\)
  • The small sample \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval for a population mean for \(\sigma\) unknown is \(\bar X \pm t_{\alpha/2} \left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\)
    • uses \(n-1\) degrees of freedom
    • the population must be normally distributed, and a sample is considered small when \(n<30\)

Exercise: Sample size and CI width

A random sample is drawn from a population with known standard deviation \(\sigma = 11.3\). The sample mean is \(\bar X = 105.2\). Not all information given need be used.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Construct a 90% CI for \(\mu\) when:
      1. \(n = 36\), \(s = 11.2\)
      1. \(n = 100\), \(s = 11.2\)
  • What happens to the width of the CI as \(n\) increases?
  • We use \(\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma / \sqrt{n}\) with \(z_{0.05} = 1.645\) and \(\sigma = 11.3\) (not \(s\), since \(\sigma\) is known)
    1. \(105.2 \pm 1.645 \times 11.3/\sqrt{36} = 105.2 \pm 3.10 = [102.10, 108.30]\)
    1. \(105.2 \pm 1.645 \times 11.3/\sqrt{100} = 105.2 \pm 1.86 = [103.34, 107.06]\)
  • The CI becomes narrower as \(n\) increases

Exercise: Interpreting a CI

A large sample yields a 95% confidence interval for the average number of cans of soda consumed per adult per year in the US: \((440, 520)\).

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • For each statement, indicate whether it is a true or false interpretation:
      1. 95% of adults in the US consume between 440 and 520 cans of soda per year
      1. there is a 95% probability that the true population mean is between 440 and 520
      1. the true population mean is between 440 and 520 cans, with 95% confidence
      1. the average soda consumption of the sampled people is between 440 and 520 cans, with 95% confidence
    1. False: the CI is about the population mean, not about individual values
    1. False: the true mean is either in the interval or not; “95%” refers to the process, not a probability for a specific interval
    1. True: this is an acceptable shorthand for saying “95% of intervals constructed this way contain the true mean”
    1. False: the sample mean \(\bar X\) is a single number (here \(\bar X = 480\)), not a range; there is no uncertainty about the sample mean
