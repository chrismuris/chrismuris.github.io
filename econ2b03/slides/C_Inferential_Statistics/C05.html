Part I: From critical values to p-values

Warm-up: what C04 already gave us

Exercise: same sample, different alpha

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Recall the machine-recalibration example from C04.
  • The test statistic was \(Z=2.49\) for a two-sided test of:
    • \(H_0:\mu=8.1\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq 8.1\)
  • What was the decision at the 1% level?
  • What would the decision be at the 5% level?
  • What does that suggest about the sample evidence?
  • At the 1% level, do not reject \(H_0\), because \(|2.49| < 2.576\).
  • At the 5% level, reject \(H_0\), because \(|2.49| > 1.96\).
  • The sample evidence is strong enough for rejection at 5%, but not strong enough for rejection at 1%.
  • That is exactly the situation the \(p\)-value summarizes in one number.

Observed significance

  • The observed significance, or \(p\)-value, measures how rare the observed test statistic would be if \(H_0\) were true.
  • More precisely:
    • it is the probability, under \(H_0\), of getting a result at least as contrary to \(H_0\) and in favor of \(H_a\) as the one actually observed
  • The \(p\)-value is computed under the null hypothesis.
  • It is not:
    • the probability that \(H_0\) is true
    • the probability that the sample happened “by accident”
    • the probability that the alternative is correct

Tail-area view

  • Computationally, the \(p\)-value is a tail area.
  • Which tail area depends on \(H_a\):
    • left-tailed test: area to the left of the observed statistic
    • right-tailed test: area to the right of the observed statistic
    • two-tailed test: twice the smaller one-tail area

Exercise: where is the p-value area?

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Suppose the observed test statistic is \(Z=-1.84\).
  • For each alternative hypothesis, state which area gives the \(p\)-value:
    • \(H_a:\mu<\mu_0\)
    • \(H_a:\mu>\mu_0\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq\mu_0\)
  • Left-tailed: the area to the left of \(-1.84\).
  • Right-tailed: the area to the right of \(-1.84\).
  • Two-tailed: twice the smaller one-tail area, here \(2P(Z\le -1.84)\).
  • The alternative hypothesis tells you which outcomes count as evidence against \(H_0\).

Key interpretation

  • The \(p\)-value is the smallest significance level at which we would reject \(H_0\).
  • Therefore:
    • smaller \(p\)-values mean stronger evidence against \(H_0\)
    • the PVA decision rule is: reject \(H_0\) if \(p \le \alpha\)
  • A useful way to read a \(p\)-value:
    • if \(p=0.032\), then we would reject at the 5% level
    • but we would not reject at the 1% level
  • So the \(p\)-value turns many possible CVA decisions into one summary number.

What a small p-value is telling you

  • A small \(p\)-value means:
    • the observed sample would be unusual if \(H_0\) were true
    • the data fit the null model poorly
  • It does not mean:
    • the null is impossible
    • the alternative is guaranteed to be correct

Part II: The p-value approach for a population mean

The PVA procedure

  • The systematic hypothesis testing procedure: p-value approach (PVA):
    1. identify \(H_0\) and \(H_a\)
    2. identify the test statistic and its distribution under \(H_0\)
    3. compute the observed test statistic
    4. compute the \(p\)-value
    5. compare \(p\) to \(\alpha\) and decide, in context

Worked example: machine recalibration revisited

Machine recalibration revisited

  • A machine is set to dispense 8.1 ounces on average.
  • The population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma=0.22\) ounce.
  • A sample of \(n=30\) containers has sample mean \(\bar X=8.2\).
  • Test whether recalibration is needed:
    • \(H_0:\mu=8.1\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq8.1\)

Step 1 to Step 3

  • The test statistic is \[Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}.\]
  • For this sample, \[Z=\frac{8.2-8.1}{0.22/\sqrt{30}} \approx 2.49.\]

Step 4: compute the p-value

  • This is a two-sided test, so we compute the upper-tail area and double it.
  • In R, that means:
    • first compute \(P(Z \ge 2.49)\)
    • then multiply by 2
1 - pnorm(2.49)
[1] 0.006387155
  • The one-tail area is about \(0.0064\).
  • Therefore the two-sided \(p\)-value is about \[2 \times 0.0064 = 0.0128.\]

Step 5: decision

  • At the 5% level:
    • \(0.0128 < 0.05\), so reject \(H_0\)
  • At the 1% level:
    • \(0.0128 > 0.01\), so do not reject \(H_0\)
  • This matches the CVA conclusions from C04 exactly.
  • The gain from the PVA is that one number lets us answer both questions.

Why CVA and PVA agree

  • The CVA asks:
    • “is the observed test statistic beyond the critical cutoff?”
  • The PVA asks:
    • “how much tail probability is beyond the observed test statistic?”
  • They are two ways of expressing the same null-distribution logic.

Worked example: NBA total score

NBA total score (SZ 8.3, Example 7)

  • Claimed mean total score is 202.5 points.
  • A sample of 85 games has mean 199.2 and standard deviation 19.63.
  • Test at the 5% level whether the true mean is lower:
    • \(H_0:\mu=202.5\)
    • \(H_a:\mu<202.5\)
  • Step 3: test statistic
  • Step 4 and Step 5
(199.2 - 202.5) / (19.63 / sqrt(85))
[1] -1.549898
  • The observed test statistic is about \(Z=-1.54\).
pnorm(-1.54)
[1] 0.06178018
  • The \(p\)-value is about \(0.0618\).
  • Since \(0.0618 > 0.05\), do not reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample suggests a lower mean, but not strongly enough for rejection at the 5% level.

Part III: Hypothesis testing for a population proportion

Large-sample test for one proportion

  • To test hypotheses about a population proportion \(p\), use \[Z=\frac{\widehat P-p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}.\]
  • Under \(H_0\), the test statistic has the standard normal distribution.
  • This is the same structure as before:
    • estimate minus hypothesized value
    • divided by a standard error computed under \(H_0\)
  • The only new ingredient is that, for proportions, the null standard error uses \(p_0\) rather than \(\widehat P\).

Example: beverage preference

Beverage preference (SZ 8.5, Example 12)

  • A soft drink maker claims that a majority of adults prefer its beverage.
  • Among 500 tasters, 270 preferred it.
  • Test at the 5% level:
    • \(H_0:p\le 0.5\)
    • \(H_a:p>0.5\)

Step 1 to Step 3

  • The sample proportion is \[\widehat P = \frac{270}{500}=0.54.\]
  • Because the alternative is \(p>0.5\), we compute the test statistic using the boundary value from the null:
    • \(p_0=0.5\)
  • Under the benchmark value \(p_0=0.5\), the test statistic is
phat <- 270 / 500
p0 <- 0.5
(phat - p0) / sqrt(p0 * (1 - p0) / 500)
[1] 1.788854

Step 4 and Step 5

  • The observed test statistic is about \(Z=1.79\).
  • This is a right-tailed test, so compute the upper-tail area.
1 - pnorm(1.79)
[1] 0.03672696
  • The \(p\)-value is about \(0.0367\).
  • Since \(0.0367 < 0.05\), reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample provides sufficient evidence at the 5% level that more than half of adults prefer the beverage.

Example: poverty rate

Poverty rate (adapted from SZ 8.5, Exercise 17)

  • The federal poverty rate is 12%.
  • In a sample of 1,550 households, 163 are classified as impoverished.
  • Test at the 5% level:
    • \(H_0:p=0.12\)
    • \(H_a:p<0.12\)
  • Step 3: test statistic
  • Step 4 and Step 5
(163 / 1550 - 0.12) / sqrt(0.12 * 0.88 / 1550)
[1] -1.797753
  • The observed test statistic is about \(Z=-1.80\).
pnorm(-1.797753)
[1] 0.03610808
  • The \(p\)-value is about \(0.0361\).
  • Since \(0.0361 < 0.05\), reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample provides sufficient evidence that the poverty rate is below 12%.

Exercises

Exercise: CVA versus PVA

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • An analyst tests \(H_0:\mu=50\) against \(H_a:\mu\neq 50\) and obtains \(Z=2.21\).
  • What is the decision at the 5% level using the CVA?
  • What is the decision at the 1% level using the CVA?
  • Without computing it exactly, where must the \(p\)-value lie?
  • At the 5% level, reject because \(|2.21| > 1.96\).
  • At the 1% level, do not reject because \(|2.21| < 2.576\).
  • The \(p\)-value must lie between 0.01 and 0.05.
  • This is why the \(p\)-value is called the smallest significance level at which rejection occurs.

Exercise (adapted from Introductory Statistics): placement exam

  • Question (4 min)
  • Answers
  • The mean score on a placement exam is 14.3.
  • A revised exam is given to 30 freshmen; the sample mean is 14.6 and the sample standard deviation is 2.4.
  • Use the PVA at the 5% level to test:
    • \(H_0:\mu=14.3\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq14.3\)
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{14.6-14.3}{2.4/\sqrt{30}} \approx 0.68.\]
  • Because this is a two-sided test and \(0.68\) is small, the \(p\)-value is much larger than 0.05.
  • Therefore, do not reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample does not provide sufficient evidence that the mean score changed.

Exercise (adapted from OpenIntro): one-sided p-value

  • Question (4 min)
  • Answers
  • A company claims that 88% of shipments arrive on time.
  • In a sample of 250 shipments, 210 arrived on time.
  • Use the PVA at the 5% level to test whether the on-time rate is lower than 88%.
  • Hypotheses:
    • \(H_0:p=0.88\)
    • \(H_a:p<0.88\)
  • Sample proportion: \[\widehat P=\frac{210}{250}=0.84.\]
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{0.84-0.88}{\sqrt{0.88(0.12)/250}} \approx -1.95.\]
  • This is a left-tailed test, so the \(p\)-value is about \(P(Z\le -1.95)\approx 0.026\).
  • Since \(0.026<0.05\), reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample provides evidence that the on-time rate is below 88%.

Exercise: interpreting a p-value

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • A two-sided hypothesis test produces a \(p\)-value of 0.083.
  • What is the decision at the 10% level?
  • What is the decision at the 5% level?
  • How strong is the evidence against \(H_0\)?
  • At the 10% level, reject \(H_0\) because \(0.083 < 0.10\).
  • At the 5% level, do not reject \(H_0\) because \(0.083 > 0.05\).
  • The evidence against \(H_0\) is moderate but not especially strong.

Exercise: reading a one-proportion test

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • A poll tests \(H_0:p=0.40\) against \(H_a:p>0.40\).
  • The observed test statistic is \(Z=1.31\).
  • Is the \(p\)-value bigger or smaller than 0.10?
  • What is the decision at the 10% level?
  • For a right-tailed test, the \(p\)-value is the area to the right of \(1.31\).
  • That area is smaller than 0.10 because the 10% right-tail cutoff is about \(1.282\).
  • Therefore, reject \(H_0\) at the 10% level.
  • This is a quick way to connect tail areas, critical values, and decisions.
