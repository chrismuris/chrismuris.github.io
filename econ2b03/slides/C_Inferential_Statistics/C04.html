Part I: Errors and significance

Two possible conclusions

  • A hypothesis test ends with one of two conclusions:
    • reject \(H_0\)
    • fail to reject \(H_0\)
  • We do not say “accept \(H_0\)”
    • the data may simply be insufficient to reject it

Type I and Type II errors

  • Two mistakes are possible in hypothesis testing:
    • a Type I error is rejecting \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is true
    • a Type II error is failing to reject \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is false

  • Courtroom analogy:
    • \(H_0\) is the presumption of innocence
    • rejecting \(H_0\) is like convicting
    • failing to reject \(H_0\) is like not convicting
  • Then:
    • Type I error = convicting an innocent person
    • Type II error = failing to convict a guilty person

Exercise: cannabis vendor and testing errors

A cannabis vendor claims that the population mean amount per unit is 1 gram. In reality, the true mean is only 0.9 gram.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Suppose your sample happens to look close enough to 1 gram that you fail to reject \(H_0:\mu=1\).
  • What type of error is this?
  • This is a Type II error.
  • The null hypothesis is false, because the true mean is 0.9 rather than 1.
  • But the sample did not provide enough evidence to reject \(H_0\).

Significance level

  • The level of significance, denoted \(\alpha\), is the probability of a Type I error.
  • In words:
    • \(\alpha\) tells us how willing we are to reject \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is actually true
  • Common choices are:
    • \(\alpha = 0.10\)
    • \(\alpha = 0.05\)
    • \(\alpha = 0.01\)
  • Smaller \(\alpha\) means:
    • a smaller Type I error probability
    • a stricter standard for rejecting \(H_0\)
    • stronger evidence required before rejection

Exercise: choosing a significance level

Two firms are considering product-quality tests.

  • In the first case, falsely rejecting a safe batch would be costly but not dangerous.
  • In the second case, falsely approving an unsafe batch could harm consumers.
  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • In which case would you want a smaller significance level?
  • What type of error are you trying hardest to avoid?
  • You would want a smaller significance level in the second case, where a false rejection of safety has more serious consequences.
  • A smaller \(\alpha\) reduces the probability of a Type I error.
  • This is the usual logic in higher-stakes testing: make it harder to reject \(H_0\) unless the evidence is strong.

Why alpha determines the rejection region

  • In C03, we said we reject \(H_0\) when the sample result would be rare if \(H_0\) were true.
  • Now we make “rare” precise:
    • an event is rare if its probability is less than \(\alpha\)
  • So once \(\alpha\) is chosen, the rejection region is the set of sample outcomes whose total probability under \(H_0\) equals \(\alpha\).

Part II: From the null hypothesis to a decision rule

First principle

  • The test procedure is built on the initial assumption that \(H_0\) is true.
  • If \(H_0:\mu=\mu_0\) is true, then:
    • the sampling distribution is centered at \(\mu_0\)
    • values of \(\bar X\) near \(\mu_0\) are unsurprising
    • values of \(\bar X\) far from \(\mu_0\) are surprising
  • The direction of “far” depends on the alternative hypothesis:
    • left-tailed: far to the left
    • right-tailed: far to the right
    • two-tailed: far in either direction

Standardized test statistic

  • To decide how unusual a sample result is, we standardize it.
  • For large-sample tests about a population mean:
    • if \(\sigma\) is known, \[Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]
    • if \(\sigma\) is unknown, \[Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\]
  • Under \(H_0\), the test statistic has a standard normal distribution.
  • Interpretation of the standardized test statistic:
    • numerator: observed sample mean minus hypothesized mean
    • denominator: standard error of the sample mean
    • so \(Z\) tells us how many standard errors the sample mean is from the null value
  • Large positive \(Z\) values are evidence for a right-tailed alternative.
  • Large negative \(Z\) values are evidence for a left-tailed alternative.
  • Large values of \(|Z|\) are evidence for a two-tailed alternative.

Rejection regions

  • Once we know the form of \(H_a\), we know where to look for evidence against \(H_0\):
    • if \(H_a:\mu<\mu_0\), reject for sufficiently small values of \(Z\)
    • if \(H_a:\mu>\mu_0\), reject for sufficiently large values of \(Z\)
    • if \(H_a:\mu\neq\mu_0\), reject for sufficiently extreme values of \(Z\) in either direction

Three critical values worth remembering

  • Left-tailed test at \(\alpha=0.05\): reject if \(Z \le -1.645\)
  • Right-tailed test at \(\alpha=0.05\): reject if \(Z \ge 1.645\)
  • Two-tailed test at \(\alpha=0.05\): reject if \(|Z| \ge 1.96\)

Exercise: identify the rejection region

Suppose you are testing the mean waiting time at a clinic against a benchmark of 18 minutes.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers

For each alternative hypothesis, state where the rejection region must lie.

  1. \(H_a:\mu<18\)
  2. \(H_a:\mu>18\)
  3. \(H_a:\mu\neq18\)
  1. For \(H_a:\mu<18\), the rejection region is in the left tail.
  2. For \(H_a:\mu>18\), the rejection region is in the right tail.
  3. For \(H_a:\mu\neq18\), the rejection region is in both tails.

The alternative hypothesis determines where evidence against \(H_0\) can appear.

Part III: The critical value approach

CVA procedure

  • The systematic hypothesis testing procedure: critical value approach (CVA):
    1. identify \(H_0\) and \(H_a\)
    2. identify the test statistic and its distribution under \(H_0\)
    3. compute the test statistic from the sample
    4. construct the rejection region using \(\alpha\)
    5. compare and decide, in context

Worked example: machine recalibration

Machine recalibration

  • A machine is set to dispense 8.1 ounces on average.
  • The population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma=0.22\) ounce.
  • A sample of \(n=30\) containers has sample mean \(\bar X=8.2\).
  • Test at the 1% level whether recalibration is needed.

Step 1: hypotheses

  • Recalibration is needed if the true mean differs from 8.1.
  • So:
    • \(H_0:\mu=8.1\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq8.1\)

Step 2: test statistic

  • Since \(\sigma\) is known, use \[Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]
  • Under \(H_0\), \(Z\) is standard normal.

Step 3: compute the test statistic

  • So the observed test statistic is approximately \(Z=2.49\).

Step 4: rejection region

  • This is a two-tailed test at the 1% level.
  • Therefore the critical values are \(\pm 2.576\).
  • Rejection region: \[Z \le -2.576 \quad \text{or} \quad Z \ge 2.576\]

Step 5: decision

  • Our observed value is \(Z=2.49\).
  • Since \(2.49\) is not in the rejection region, we do not reject \(H_0\).
  • Contextual conclusion:
    • At the 1% significance level, the sample does not provide sufficient evidence that the machine’s mean fill amount differs from 8.1 ounces.

Why this makes sense

  • The sample mean is above 8.1, so there is some evidence against \(H_0\).
  • But at the 1% level, we require very strong evidence.
  • A test statistic of 2.49 standard errors is large, but not quite large enough for that stricter cutoff.

A second worked example: one-tailed test

Growth hormone for cows

  • A sample of 100 cows has mean growth 38.3 inches.
  • Historical baseline mean is 36 inches.
  • Assume \(\sigma=5.6\) inches.
  • Test whether growth hormone increases growth rate:
    • \(H_0:\mu=36\)
    • \(H_a:\mu>36\)
  • By hand
  • In R
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{38.3-36}{5.6/\sqrt{100}} \approx 4.11\]
  • Right-tailed rejection region at \(\alpha=0.05\): \[Z \ge 1.645\]
  • Since \(4.11 > 1.645\), reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample provides strong evidence that mean growth is higher than 36 inches.

  • R calculation:

    (38.3 - 36) / (5.6 / sqrt(100))

  • Output: 4.107143

Worked example (adapted from OpenIntro): textbook prices

  • A campus bookstore claims the mean price of introductory textbooks is $90.
  • A sample of \(n=64\) textbooks has mean price \(\bar X=\$94.20\).
  • Assume the population standard deviation is \(\sigma=\$16\).
  • Test at the 5% level whether the mean price is higher than $90.
  • Setup
  • Solution
  • Hypotheses:
    • \(H_0:\mu=90\)
    • \(H_a:\mu>90\)
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]
  • Compute \[Z=\frac{94.2-90}{16/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{4.2}{2}=2.10.\]
  • Right-tailed critical value at \(\alpha=0.05\): \[Z \ge 1.645.\]
  • Since \(2.10>1.645\), reject \(H_0\).
  • At the 5% level, the sample provides sufficient evidence that the mean textbook price exceeds $90.

Exercises

Exercise: repair times

The standard time for a repair is 1 hour. A sample of 30 repairs has mean 0.86 hour and standard deviation 0.32 hour.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Use the critical value approach at the 1% level to test:
    • \(H_0:\mu=1\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq1\)
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{0.86-1}{0.32/\sqrt{30}} \approx -2.40\]
  • Two-tailed rejection region at \(\alpha=0.01\): \[|Z| \ge 2.576\]
  • Since \(|-2.40| < 2.576\), do not reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample suggests a lower repair time, but not strongly enough for rejection at the 1% level.

Exercise (adapted from Introductory Statistics): placement exam

The historical mean score on a placement exam is 14.3. A sample of 30 students taking a revised version has mean 14.6 and sample standard deviation 2.4.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Use the critical value approach at the 5% level to test:
    • \(H_0:\mu=14.3\)
    • \(H_a:\mu\neq14.3\)
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{14.6-14.3}{2.4/\sqrt{30}} \approx 0.68.\]
  • Two-tailed rejection region at \(\alpha=0.05\): \[|Z| \ge 1.96.\]
  • Since \(|0.68| < 1.96\), do not reject \(H_0\).
  • The revised exam sample does not provide enough evidence that the mean score differs from 14.3.

Exercise: respirator testing

A manufacturer claims its respirator delivers pure air for 75 minutes on average. A regulator is concerned that the true mean may be lower. In a large sample, the standardized test statistic is \(Z=-1.91\).

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Test at the 5% level:
    • \(H_0:\mu=75\)
    • \(H_a:\mu<75\)
  • What is the rejection region, and what is the decision?
  • This is a left-tailed test at \(\alpha=0.05\).
  • Rejection region: \[Z \le -1.645\]
  • Since \(-1.91 < -1.645\), reject \(H_0\).
  • There is sufficient evidence at the 5% level that the mean air-delivery time is below 75 minutes.

Exercise (adapted from OpenIntro): significance and decisions

An analyst tests \(H_0:\mu=50\) against \(H_a:\mu\neq50\) and computes the test statistic \(Z=2.21\).

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. What is the decision at the 5% level?
  2. What is the decision at the 1% level?
  3. What lesson does this illustrate about the significance level?
  1. At the 5% level, the two-tailed critical value is \(1.96\), so reject \(H_0\).
  2. At the 1% level, the two-tailed critical value is \(2.576\), so do not reject \(H_0\).
  3. A smaller significance level makes the test more conservative: the same sample can lead to different decisions depending on how much Type I error risk we are willing to tolerate.
