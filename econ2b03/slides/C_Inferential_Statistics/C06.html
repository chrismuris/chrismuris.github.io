Part I: Why two-population inference matters

Economic motivation

  • Many empirical questions are comparative:
    • Did a policy change outcomes?
    • Do two groups differ?
    • Did performance improve after an intervention?
  • That means the target parameter is often a difference:
    • \(\mu_1-\mu_2\)
    • or \(p_1-p_2\)

Two basic sampling designs

  • Independent samples:
    • sample 1 is drawn without reference to sample 2
    • example: treatment schools versus control schools
  • Paired samples:
    • each observation in sample 1 is naturally matched to one in sample 2
    • example: before/after for the same unit, or matched applicants in an audit study

Part II: Independent samples and differences in means

The framework

  • Suppose:
    • sample 1 has size \(n_1\), mean \(\bar X_1\), standard deviation \(s_1\)
    • sample 2 has size \(n_2\), mean \(\bar X_2\), standard deviation \(s_2\)
  • The natural point estimate of the population difference is \[\bar X_1-\bar X_2.\]

Confidence interval for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\)

  • For large, independent samples, the confidence interval is \[\bar X_1-\bar X_2 \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}.\]
  • The logic is familiar:
    • estimate \(\pm\) critical value times standard error
  • Key fact to remember:
    • for independent samples, the variance of the difference is the sum of the variances
  • The most common bookkeeping error is the subtraction order.
  • If you define the parameter as \(\mu_1-\mu_2\), then:
    • the estimate must be \(\bar X_1-\bar X_2\)
    • the hypotheses must use the same order
    • the interpretation of the sign must use the same order

Worked example: cable TV satisfaction

Cable TV satisfaction (SZ 9.1, Example 1)

  • To compare customer satisfaction for two cable companies:
    • Company 1: \(n_1=174\), \(\bar X_1=3.51\), \(s_1=0.51\)
    • Company 2: \(n_2=355\), \(\bar X_2=3.24\), \(s_2=0.52\)
  • Construct a point estimate and a 99% confidence interval for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\).
  • Point estimate
  • Confidence interval
  • The point estimate is \[\bar X_1-\bar X_2=3.51-3.24=0.27.\]
  • For a 99% CI, use \(z_{\alpha/2}=2.576\).
  • Margin of error:
2.576 * sqrt(0.51^2/174 + 0.52^2/355)
[1] 0.1223673
  • So the CI is
0.27 + 2.576 * sqrt(0.51^2/174 + 0.52^2/355) * c(-1, 1)
[1] 0.1476327 0.3923673
  • Interpretation: customers of Company 1 appear to rate it between about 0.11 and 0.43 points higher on average.

Hypothesis test for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\)

  • For large, independent samples, test hypotheses about \[H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=D_0\] using \[Z=\frac{(\bar X_1-\bar X_2)-D_0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}.\]
  • Under \(H_0\), \(Z\) is standard normal.

Worked example: football offense

Football offense (adapted from SZ 9.1, Exercise 23)

  • An owner believes the league has become more offense-oriented.
  • Compare points per game from two eras:
    • earlier sample: \(n_1=32\), \(\bar X_1=20.62\), \(s_1=4.17\)
    • later sample: \(n_2=32\), \(\bar X_2=22.05\), \(s_2=4.01\)
  • Test at the 10% level whether the later era has a higher mean score.

Step 1 and Step 2

  • Let population 1 be the earlier era and population 2 be the later era.
  • Then
    • \(H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0\)
    • \(H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2<0\)
  • Use the large-sample test statistic for independent samples.

Step 3 to Step 5

(20.62 - 22.05) / sqrt(4.17^2/32 + 4.01^2/32)
[1] -1.398266
  • The observed test statistic is about \(Z=-1.40\).
  • At the 10% level for a left-tailed test, the critical value is about \(-1.282\).
  • Since \(-1.40 < -1.282\), reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample provides sufficient evidence, at the 10% level, that the later era has a higher mean score.

Exercise: interpreting the sign of \(\bar X_1-\bar X_2\)

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Suppose population 1 is the control group and population 2 is the treatment group.
  • If \(\bar X_1-\bar X_2\) is negative, which group has the larger sample mean?
  • If your alternative is that treatment increases outcomes, should you test \(H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2<0\) or \(H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2>0\)?
  • A negative value means sample 2, the treatment group, has the larger sample mean.
  • If treatment is population 2, then treatment increases outcomes corresponds to \(H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2<0\).
  • A lot of mistakes in two-sample inference come from losing track of the order of subtraction.

Part III: Paired samples

Why pairing changes the problem

  • With paired data, the two observations in a pair are linked.
  • So we should not pretend the samples were independent.
  • Instead:
    • compute the difference within each pair
    • then analyze the single sample of differences

The sample of differences

  • Let the pairwise differences be \[d_1,d_2,\ldots,d_n.\]
  • Then define:
    • \(\bar d\) = sample mean of the differences
    • \(s_d\) = sample standard deviation of the differences
  • The target parameter is \[\mu_d=\mu_1-\mu_2.\]

Confidence interval for paired samples

  • The confidence interval for paired data is \[\bar d \pm t_{\alpha/2}\frac{s_d}{\sqrt n}.\]
  • Degrees of freedom: \[df=n-1.\]
  • This should feel familiar:
    • paired inference is really a one-sample inference problem applied to the differences
  • Pairing helps when the match explains part of the variation.
  • By taking within-pair differences, we remove pair-specific noise and focus on the treatment or timing contrast.

Worked example: fuel economy

Fuel economy (SZ 9.3, Example 7)

  • Nine matched pairs of vehicles are tested with two gasoline formulations.
  • The pairwise differences in fuel economy are \[d=(0,0.3,-0.1,0.4,0.2,0.3,0.0,0.1,0.1).\]
  • Construct a 95% confidence interval for the mean difference.
  • Ingredients
  • Confidence interval
d <- c(0, 0.3, -0.1, 0.4, 0.2, 0.3, 0.0, 0.1, 0.1)
mean(d)
[1] 0.1444444
sd(d)
[1] 0.1666667
  • So \(\bar d \approx 0.144\) and \(s_d \approx 0.167\).
  • Since \(n=9\), use a \(t\) critical value with \(df=8\).
mean(d) + qt(0.025, df = 8) * sd(d) / sqrt(9) * c(1, -1)
[1] 0.0163331 0.2725558
  • The interval is approximately \((0.016, 0.273)\).
  • Since the interval is positive, it suggests formulation 1 yields higher fuel economy on average.

Hypothesis test for paired samples

  • Test hypotheses about \(\mu_d\) using \[T=\frac{\bar d-D_0}{s_d/\sqrt n}.\]
  • Degrees of freedom are \(n-1\).

Worked example: house appraisals

House appraisals (adapted from SZ 9.3, Exercise 7)

  • For 10 houses, compare county-government appraisals to private-company appraisals.
  • Take differences as \[d=\text{county} - \text{private} = (-2,12,5,0,2,3,18,2,-8,0).\]
  • Test at the 1% level whether government appraisals are higher on average.

Step 1 to Step 3

  • Because differences are defined as county minus private:
    • \(H_0:\mu_d=0\)
    • \(H_a:\mu_d>0\)
d <- c(-2, 12, 5, 0, 2, 3, 18, 2, -8, 0)
mean(d)
[1] 3.2
sd(d)
[1] 7.269418
(mean(d) - 0) / (sd(d) / sqrt(length(d)))
[1] 1.392035

Step 4 and Step 5

  • Here \(n=10\), so \(df=9\).
  • For a right-tailed test at the 1% level, the critical value is
qt(0.99, df = 9)
[1] 2.821438
  • The observed test statistic is about \(T=1.39\).
  • The critical value is about \(2.82\).
  • Since \(1.39 < 2.82\), do not reject \(H_0\).
  • The sample does not provide sufficient evidence, at the 1% level, that government appraisals are higher.

Exercise: choosing the paired difference

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • A study records each worker’s earnings before training and after training.
  • You want positive differences to mean improvement.
  • How should you define the pairwise difference?
  • If training is effective, what sign should \(\bar d\) have?
  • Define the difference as after - before.
  • Then positive differences correspond to higher earnings after training.
  • If training is effective, \(\bar d\) should be positive.
  • This sign choice should be made before computing anything else.

Part IV: Differences in proportions

Confidence interval for \(p_1-p_2\)

  • For two independent samples, the confidence interval is \[\widehat P_1-\widehat P_2 \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\widehat P_1(1-\widehat P_1)}{n_1}+\frac{\widehat P_2(1-\widehat P_2)}{n_2}}.\]

Hypothesis test for \(p_1-p_2\)

  • For testing \(H_0:p_1-p_2=0\), first compute the pooled estimate \[\widehat P=\frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2}.\]
  • Then use the large-sample test statistic \[Z=\frac{\widehat P_1-\widehat P_2}{\sqrt{\widehat P(1-\widehat P)\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}.\]
  • This differs from the confidence-interval formula.
  • For the test, the null says the two population proportions are the same, so we pool under that null benchmark.

Exercise: comparing machines

  • Question (4 min)
  • Answers
  • In a sample of 400 products from machine 1, 23 were defective.
  • In a sample of 400 products from machine 2, 17 were defective.
  • Test at the 5% level:
    • \(H_0:p_1-p_2=0\)
    • \(H_a:p_1-p_2\neq 0\)
  • Sample proportions:
    • \(\widehat P_1=23/400=0.0575\)
    • \(\widehat P_2=17/400=0.0425\)
  • Pooled proportion: \[\widehat P=\frac{23+17}{400+400}=0.05.\]
  • Pooled standard error: \[\sqrt{0.05(0.95)\left(\frac{1}{400}+\frac{1}{400}\right)} \approx 0.0154.\]
  • Test statistic: \[Z=\frac{0.0575-0.0425}{0.0154}\approx 0.97.\]
  • For a two-sided test at 5%, the critical values are \(\pm 1.96\).
  • Since \(|0.97|<1.96\), do not reject \(H_0\).
  • The observed difference in defect rates is not statistically significant at the 5% level.

Worked example: code enforcement

Code enforcement (adapted from SZ 9.4, Examples 10 and 11)

  • Before web publication of inspection records:
    • \(n_1=500\), \(\widehat P_1=0.67\)
  • After web publication:
    • \(n_2=100\), \(\widehat P_2=0.80\)
  • Construct a 90% confidence interval for \(p_1-p_2\).
  • Point estimate
  • Confidence interval
  • The point estimate is \[\widehat P_1-\widehat P_2=0.67-0.80=-0.13.\]
p1_hat <- 0.67
p2_hat <- 0.80
-0.13 + 1.645 * sqrt(p1_hat*(1-p1_hat)/500 + p2_hat*(1-p2_hat)/100) * c(-1, 1)
[1] -0.20433871 -0.05566129
  • The 90% confidence interval is about \((-0.20,-0.06)\).
  • Interpretation: the proportion passing on the first inspection appears to be between 6 and 20 percentage points higher after publication.
