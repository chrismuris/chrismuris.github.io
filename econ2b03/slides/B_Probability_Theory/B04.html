Part I: Binomial Random Variables

Goal: Counting successes

  • Many economic variables are counts of “successes” in a sample:
    • number of unemployed workers in a survey of 1,000
    • number of “yes” votes in a poll
    • number of defaults in a loan portfolio
  • We model these using the binomial distribution.
  • The “building block” of this distribution is a sequence of independent trials called a Bernoulli process.

Bernoulli process

  • A Bernoulli process with parameters \(n\) and \(p\) consists of a sequence of \(n\) identical trials of a random experiment such that each trial:
    • produces one of two possible complementary outcomes that are conventionally called success, with probability \(p\) and failure with probability \(q=1-p\)
    • stands independent of any other trial
    • the probability of success on any one trial is the same number \(p\)
    • what happens on one trial has nothing to do with what happens on any other trial

Binomial random variable

  • A discrete random variable \(X\) that counts the number of successes in a Bernoulli process with parameters \(n\) and \(p\) is the binomial random variable with parameters \(n\) and \(p\).
  • Possible values (support):
    • \(X \in \{0, 1, 2, \dots, n\}\)
  • Notation:
    • \(X \sim \text{Binom}(n, p)\)
  • Parameters:
    • \(n\): number of trials (must be a positive integer)
    • \(p\): probability of success (between 0 and 1)
  • We also say that \(X\) has a binomial distribution with parameters \(n\) and \(p\).

Bernoulli examples (SZ 4.3)

  • Number of boys in a family of 3: \(n=3, p=0.5\)
  • Number of heads in 20 coin flips: \(n=20, p=0.5\)
  • Number of female students in a sample of 125, from a population that is 57% female: \(n=125, p=0.57\)
  • Correct answers on a 15-question multiple choice test (4 options each), guessing randomly: \(n=15, p=0.25\)

Spend some time thinking about example 3 (sampling from a population) to prep for Part C.

The binomial probability distribution

  • The binomial probability distribution is a list of each possible value of a binomial random variable \(X\) together with the probability that \(X\) takes that value.
  • If \(X\) is a binomial random variable with parameters \(n\) and \(p\), then \[P(x)= \binom{n}{x} p^x q^{n-x},\] where \(q=1-p\).
  • Recall: \(\binom{n}{x}\) means
    • \(\binom{n}{x} = \frac{n!}{x!(n-x)!}\) (i.e., number of combinations)
    • \(n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times 1\)

Smokers in a sample of six

  • Suppose 40% of the population are smokers (\(p=0.4\)). Find the probability that 2 out of 6 randomly selected people smoke.
  • \(n=6, x=2, p=0.4, q=0.6\): \[P(X=2) = \binom{6}{2} 0.4^2 0.6^{4} = 15 \times 0.16 \times 0.1296 = 0.311\]
dbinom(2, 6, 0.4)
[1] 0.31104

Loan defaults in a small portfolio

  • A bank holds 10 small-business loans. Historical data show that each loan independently defaults with probability \(p=0.05\).
  • Let \(X\) = number of defaults. Then \(X \sim \text{Binom}(n=10, p=0.05)\).
  • Probability of no defaults: \[P(X=0) = \binom{10}{0} 0.05^0 \, 0.95^{10} = 0.95^{10} \approx 0.5987\]
  • Probability of exactly one default: \[P(X=1) = \binom{10}{1} 0.05^1 \, 0.95^{9} = 10 \times 0.05 \times 0.6302 \approx 0.3151\]
dbinom(0, 10, 0.05)
[1] 0.5987369
dbinom(1, 10, 0.05)
[1] 0.3151247

Computing binomial probabilities

  • We can obtain binomial probabilities from:
    • the binomial distribution function (compute it!)
    • binomial tables in SZ appendix
    • R, using dbinom() or pbinom()
    • a McMaster-approved calculator
  • Use whichever method is most convenient for the task.
  • On a midterm, you may need to compute binomial probabilities by hand on your calculator
  • Tip: simplify the binomial coefficient before punching numbers
    • \(\binom{100}{2} = \frac{100!}{98!\,2!} = \frac{100 \times 99}{2} = 4{,}950\)
    • most factorials cancel; only a few terms remain
  • Practice computing \(P(X=2)\) for \(X \sim \text{Bin}(100, 0.25)\) on your McMaster calculator

Part II: Mean and Variance

Mean and variance of a binomial RV

  • What is the mean of a binomial RV with \(n=125\) and \(p=0.56\)?
  • Using the definition: \(E(X) = \sum_{x=0}^{125} x \binom{125}{x} 0.56^x 0.44^{125-x}\) — painful!
  • Luckily, there is a shortcut.
  • The mean of a binomial RV with parameters \(n\) and \(p\) is \[\mu = n p\]
  • The variance is \[\sigma^2 = n p q = n p (1-p)\]
  • The average number of successes is the number of trials times the probability of success; the variance also depends on the probability of failure (\(q\))

Boys in a family of three

  • \(X \sim \text{Binom}(n=3, p=0.5)\)
  • Mean: \(\mu = np = 3 \times 0.5 = 1.5\)
  • Variance: \(\sigma^2 = npq = 3 \times 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75\)
  • Standard deviation: \(\sigma = \sqrt{0.75} \approx 0.87\)

Smokers in a sample of six (continued)

  • Recall: \(X \sim \text{Binom}(n=6, p=0.4)\)
  • Mean: \(\mu = 6 \times 0.4 = 2.4\)
  • Variance: \(\sigma^2 = 6 \times 0.4 \times 0.6 = 1.44\)
  • Standard deviation: \(\sigma = 1.2\)

Defective items on an assembly line

  • A factory inspects \(n=200\) items from a production run. Each item independently has a 3% chance of being defective (\(p=0.03\)).
  • Let \(X\) = number of defective items. Then \(X \sim \text{Binom}(200, 0.03)\).
  • Mean: \(\mu = 200 \times 0.03 = 6\)
    • on average, 6 out of 200 items are defective
  • Standard deviation: \(\sigma = \sqrt{200 \times 0.03 \times 0.97} \approx 2.41\)
    • the number of defectives typically varies by about 2.4 items from the mean
n <- 200; p <- 0.03
n * p
[1] 6
sqrt(n * p * (1 - p))
[1] 2.412468

Exercise: True/false exam guessing

You take a ten-question true/false exam and guess on every question.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. Find the probability that you get exactly six questions right.
  2. Find the probability that you get a passing grade (60% or greater).
  1. \(X\) is binomial with \(n=10, p=0.5\). \[P(6) = \binom{10}{6} 0.5^6 0.5^4 \approx 0.2051\] In R: dbinom(6, 10, 0.5)
  2. Passing is \(P(X \ge 6) = 1 - P(X \le 5)\). From the table: \(1 - 0.6230 = 0.3770\) In R: 1 - pbinom(5, 10, 0.5)

Exercise: Broken eggs

The probability that an egg in a carton of 12 is broken is 0.025.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. Find the probability that a carton of 12 eggs contains no broken eggs.
  2. Find the probability that a carton of 12 eggs has at least one broken egg.
  3. Find the average number of broken eggs in 12 cartons (144 eggs total).
  • Assume independence; \(X\) is binomial with \(p=0.025\).
  1. \(P(X=0) = \binom{12}{0} 0.025^0 0.975^{12} \approx 0.7386\)
  2. \(P(X \ge 1) = 1 - P(0) \approx 1 - 0.7386 = 0.2614\)
  3. \(E(X) = n p = 144 \times 0.025 = 3.6\)

Exercise: Mortgage approvals

A bank approves 70% of mortgage applications that meet its basic criteria. In a batch of 15 applications that meet the basic criteria, let \(X\) be the number that are approved. Assume applications are evaluated independently.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. What are the parameters of \(X\)?
  2. Find the mean and standard deviation of \(X\).
  3. Find the probability that exactly 10 applications are approved.
  1. \(X \sim \text{Binom}(n=15, p=0.70)\)
  2. Mean and standard deviation:
    • \(\mu = np = 15 \times 0.70 = 10.5\)
    • \(\sigma = \sqrt{npq} = \sqrt{15 \times 0.70 \times 0.30} = \sqrt{3.15} \approx 1.77\)
  3. Probability of exactly 10 approvals: \[P(X=10) = \binom{15}{10} 0.70^{10} \, 0.30^{5} = 3003 \times 0.0282 \times 0.00243 \approx 0.2061\]
n <- 15; p <- 0.70
n * p
[1] 10.5
sqrt(n * p * (1 - p))
[1] 1.774824
dbinom(10, 15, 0.70)
[1] 0.2061304
