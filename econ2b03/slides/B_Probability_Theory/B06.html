Part I: The standard normal distribution

Definition

  • A standard normal random variable is a normally distributed random variable with mean \(\mu=0\) and standard deviation \(\sigma=1\)
  • In this course, whenever you see a \(Z\), you may assume that \(Z\sim N(0,1)\)

Computing probabilities for standard normal RVs

  • It will be very important to compute probabilities of the form:
    1. \(P(Z<a)\)
    2. \(P(Z>b)\)
    3. \(P(a<Z<b)\)
    • 2 and 3 are based on 1

  • First, computing \[P(Z<a),\] say \(P(Z<1.48)\)

  • Mathematically: \[P(Z<a) = \int_{-\infty}^a \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\times e^{-\frac{x^2}{2}},\] which turns out to be too hard to do in practice

  • You have at least three alternatives:

    1. Read probabilities from Figure 12.2
    2. Use R
    3. Use the empirical rule if \(a \in \{-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3\}\)

The first method: tables

  • The first method involves tables in the Appendix:

Table lookup for \(Z=1.48\)

  • For \(P(Z<1.48)=P(Z\leq 1.48)\) we need the one on the right:

Table entry for \(Z=1.48\)

  • \(P(Z<1.48) = P(Z\leq 1.48) = 0.9306\)

Computing \(P(Z>b)\)

  • That’s \(P(Z<a)\). How about \(P(Z>b)\)?
  • Use the rule of complements \[P(Z>b) = 1-P(Z\leq b)\] and look up \(P(Z\leq b)\) in the same table
    • event \(A = B^c = \{Z > b\}\) is complement of \(B = \{Z \leq b \}\)
    • recall that \(P(B) + P(B^c) = 1\)
    • study Example 5 in readings

Computing \(P(a<Z<b)\)

  • Consider the events
    • \(A_1\), that \(Z<a\)
    • \(A_2\), that \(Z<b\)
    • \(B\), that \(a<Z<b\)
  • Observe that
    • \(A_1 \cup B = A_2\) (right?)
    • \(A_1\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive (right?)
  • Use our rules for working with probabilities of events: \[P(A_1 \cup B) = P(A_1) + P(B) = P(A_2)\] and we can conclude \(P(B) = P(A_2) - P(A_1)\), in other words \[P(a<Z<b) = P(Z<b) - P(Z<a).\]

Table lookup for an interval

  • Example (from the text): \(P(0.50 < Z < 1.57)\)
  • Look up \(P(Z<1.57)=0.9418\) and \(P(Z<0.50)=0.6915\)
  • Subtract: \(0.9418-0.6915=0.2503\)

Interval probabilities with the Z table

  • Example 6(a): \(P(0.50<Z<1.57)\)
    • \(P(Z<1.57)=0.9418\), \(P(Z<0.50)=0.6915\)
    • \(P(0.50<Z<1.57)=0.9418-0.6915=0.2503\)

Part II: Probabilities for any normal distribution

Standardization

  • We are moving from the standard normal \(N(0,1)\) to the general case \(N(\mu,\sigma)\), and standardization is the bridge
  • Recall that \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) or \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma)\) means that \(X\) is a normal RV with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\)
  • How to compute
    1. \(P(X<a)\)?
    2. \(P(X>b)\)?
    3. \(P(a<X<b)\)?
  • We will focus on the hardest one (3)
  • Standardization: if \[X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\] is any normal RV then \[Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\] is a standard normal RV
  • We can use this via \[\begin{align*} P(a<X<b) &= P\left( \frac{a-\mu}{\sigma} < \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} < \frac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\right) \\ &= P\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\sigma} < Z <\frac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\right), \end{align*}\]
  • Then do computations for \(Z\) to get answers for \(X\)
  • Suppose \(X\sim N(70,10)\) and we want \(P(60<X<85)\)
  • Standardize the endpoints:
    • \(z_L=(60-70)/10=-1.00\)
    • \(z_U=(85-70)/10=1.50\)
  • Convert to a \(Z\) probability:
    • \(P(60<X<85)=P(-1.00<Z<1.50)\)
    • Look up \(P(Z<1.50)\) and \(P(Z<-1.00)\), then subtract

Tire tread lifetimes (continued)

  • The lifetimes of the tread of a certain automobile tire are normally distributed with mean 37,500 miles and standard deviation 4,500 miles. Find the probability that the tread life of a randomly selected tire will be between 30,000 and 40,000 miles.

  • Step 1: Standardize the bounds.
    • \(z_L=(30000-37500)/4500\approx -1.67\)
    • \(z_U=(40000-37500)/4500\approx 0.56\)
  • Step 2: Look up the left tails in the \(Z\) table.
    • \(P(Z<0.56)=0.7123\)
    • \(P(Z<-1.67)=0.0475\)
  • Step 3: Subtract to get the interval probability.
    • \(P(30000<X<40000)\approx 0.7123-0.0475=0.6648\)

Exercise: Blood pressure (SZ 5.3, 13)

The systolic blood pressure \(X\) of adults in a region is normal with mean 112 mm Hg and standard deviation 15 mm Hg.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • A person is prehypertensive if \(120<X<130\). Find this probability
  • \(X \sim N(112,15)\)

pnorm(130, mean = 112, sd = 15) - pnorm(120, mean = 112, sd = 15)

Part III: Using R

Using R for normal probabilities

  • This is the approach when you have a computer, for assignments or labs
  • It is not meant for midterms or closed book settings
  • The goal is the same: compute \(P(X \le x)\) accurately and quickly
  • Key idea: tables give \(\Phi(z)\), pnorm gives \(\Phi(z)\) directly

Standard normal in R

  • Use pnorm for left tail probabilities when \(Z\sim N(0,1)\)
  • Example: compare with the table value \(P(Z<-1.67)=0.0475\)
pnorm(-1.67)
[1] 0.04745968

Tire tread lifetimes (continued, in R)

  • If \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma)\), you can compute probabilities directly
  • Tire tread example: \(P(30000<X<40000)\) in one line
pnorm(40000, mean = 37500, sd = 4500) -
  pnorm(30000, mean = 37500, sd = 4500)
[1] 0.6629523
  • Optional check: standardize first and get the same answer
pnorm((40000 - 37500)/4500) - pnorm((30000 - 37500)/4500)
[1] 0.6629523
  • Comparison to table-based computation:
    • From tables: \(P(Z<0.56)=0.7123\) and \(P(Z<-1.67)=0.0475\)
    • Table result: \(0.7123-0.0475=0.6648\)
    • R result should match up to rounding

Hourly wages (R example)

  • Hourly wages are approximately \(N(28, 6^2)\) (mean CAD 28, sd CAD 6)
  • Find the fraction of workers earning less than CAD 20 per hour
pnorm(20, mean = 28, sd = 6)
[1] 0.09121122

SAT scores (R example)

  • SAT scores are approximately \(N(1500, 300^2)\) (mean 1500, sd 300)
  • Find the fraction of students scoring below 1200
pnorm(1200, mean = 1500, sd = 300)
[1] 0.1586553

Exercise: Normal probabilities (SZ 5.3, 5)

Let \(X\) be normal with mean 500 and standard deviation 25.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Find \(P(X < 400)\)
  • Find \(P(466<X<625)\)
  • \(P(X<400)=P(Z<-4)=0.00003\) (essentially zero)
  • \(466 < X < 625 \Leftrightarrow -34/25 < Z < 5\)
  • \(P(Z<5)\) is essentially 1 and \(P(Z<-1.36)=0.0869\)
  • Result: \(P(466<X<625)\approx 1-0.0869=0.9131\)

Exercise: Symmetry (SZ 5.1, adapted)

A continuous random variable \(X\) has a normal distribution with mean 73. The probability that \(X\) takes a value greater than 80 is 0.212.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Use symmetry to find \(P(X<66)\)
  • Sketch the density curve with the two relevant tails shaded
  • 80 is 7 above the mean, and 66 is 7 below, so the two tails are equal by symmetry
  • \(P(X<66)=0.212\)

Exercise: Right tail cutoff (SZ 5.4, adapted)

Use the standard normal table to find the value \(z^*\) such that \(P(Z>z^*)=0.025\).

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers

Exercise: Portfolio returns

A stock portfolio has annual returns that are approximately normal with mean 14.7% and standard deviation 33%.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  1. What percent of years does the portfolio lose money (return < 0%)?
  2. What is the cutoff for the highest 15% of returns?
  1. Standardize: \(z = (0 - 14.7)/33 = -0.445\). \(P(Z < -0.445) \approx 0.328\). About 33% of years the portfolio loses money.
  2. The highest 15% means a right tail of 0.15, so the left tail is 0.85. \(z_{0.85} \approx 1.04\). Cutoff: \(x^* = 14.7 + 33 \times 1.04 = 49.0\%\).
pnorm(0, mean = 14.7, sd = 33)
[1] 0.3279957
qnorm(0.85, mean = 14.7, sd = 33)
[1] 48.9023
