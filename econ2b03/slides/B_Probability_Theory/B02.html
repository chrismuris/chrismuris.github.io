Part I: Conditional Probability

Why conditional probability?

  • Many economic questions are conditional:
    • unemployment given education
    • default risk given income
    • turnout given age
  • Conditional probability is the precursor to conditional expectation:
    • it describes how the distribution of one variable changes as we condition on another
  • This is foundational for empirical analysis, including regression.

Unconditional, joint, and conditional

  • First, we distinguish between:
    1. unconditional
    2. joint
    3. conditional
  • These concepts only make sense if there are at least two events.
  • The unconditional probability \(p(A)\) is the likelihood that a particular event will occur, regardless of whether another event occurs.
    • the chance that \(A\) happens, ignoring everything else
  • The joint probability \(p(A \cap B)\) is the likelihood that two or more events will occur simultaneously, or jointly.
    • it is the probability of the intersection, probability of \(A\) and \(B\)
    • the chance that both \(A\) and \(B\) happen together
  • The conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\), denoted \(p(A|B)\), is the probability that event \(A\) has occurred in a trial of a random experiment for which it is known that event \(B\) has definitely occurred.
    • it may be computed using the formula \[p(A|B)=\frac{p(A\cap B)}{p(B)}.\]
    • restrict attention to trials where \(B\) happened, then compute how often \(A\) happens within that restricted group

Card deck

Card deck (continued)

  • Unconditional: drawing a heart (event \(B\)) \[p(\text{heart})=p(B)=\frac{13}{52}\]
  • Joint: drawing a face card (event \(A\)) and a heart (event \(B\)) \[p(\text{face card}\cap\text{heart})=p(A\cap B)=\frac{3}{52}\]
  • Conditional: drawing a face card (event \(A\)) given the card is a heart (event \(B\)) \[\begin{aligned} p(\text{face card}|\text{heart})&=p(A|B)\\ &=\frac{p(A\cap B)}{p(B)}\\ &=\frac{3/52}{13/52}\\ &=\frac{3}{13} \end{aligned}\]

Conditioning on an odd roll

  • A fair die is rolled. Initially, \(P(\text{five})=1/6\).
  • You then learn the outcome was odd. The possible outcomes are now \(\{1,3,5\}\), so \[P(\text{five} \mid \text{odd}) = 1/3.\]
  • Interpretation: new information changes the probability.

Unemployment and education

  • \(U\) = unemployed, \(C\) = has a college degree

  • \(P(U)=0.08\), \(P(C)=0.40\), \(P(U \cap C)=0.02\)

  • Then \[P(U \mid C)=\frac{P(U \cap C)}{P(C)}=\frac{0.02}{0.40}=0.05.\]

  • Interpretation: unemployment is lower among college graduates in this example.

Health insurance and health status

  • A survey of adults classifies respondents by health coverage and health status.
  • Joint probability table:
Excellent Not excellent Total
Insured 0.21 0.66 0.87
Uninsured 0.02 0.11 0.13
Total 0.23 0.77 1.00
  • \(P(\text{excellent} \mid \text{insured}) = 0.21/0.87 = 0.241\)
  • \(P(\text{excellent} \mid \text{uninsured}) = 0.02/0.13 = 0.154\)
  • Insured individuals are more likely to report excellent health, though this does not establish causation.

Exercise: Conditional probability for a standard deck

You draw one card from a standard 52-card deck.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • What is the probability of drawing a card with an even number, given that you drew a number card with a value of 8 or higher?
  • \(A\) is “number card of 8 or higher”: there are 12 such cards so \(P(A) = 12/52\)
  • \(B\) is “even number card”: there are 4*5 such cards, so \(P(B) = 20/52\)
    • But \(P(B)\) is irrelevant!
  • \(A\) and \(B\): an even number card of 8 or higher: there are 8 such cards, so \(P(A \text{ and } B) = 8/52\)
  • \(p(B|A) = p(A \text{ and } B)/p(A) = (8 / 52) / ( 12 / 52) = 8 / 12 = 2/3\)

Part II: Collectively Exhaustive Events

Collectively exhaustive events

  • A set of events is called collectively exhaustive if the union contains all outcomes in the sample space.
  • On any trial, at least one of these events is bound to occur.
  • Leading example: the events \(A\) and \(A^c\) are:
    • mutually exclusive
    • collectively exhaustive
    • exactly one of \(A\), \(A^c\) happens.

Suits as a partition

  • The random experiment is to draw a card at random from a standard deck.
  • The outcome corresponds to the suit:
    • \(B_1\) is the event of drawing a heart
    • \(B_2\) is the event of drawing a club
    • \(B_3\) is the event of a spade
    • \(B_4\) the event of a diamond.
  • The events \(B_1,B_2,B_3,B_4\) are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.

Dice events

  • Random experiment: throwing a die.
  • Sample space: \(\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\).
  • Events:
    • Let \(A\) be the event of rolling an even number,
    • \(B\) the event of rolling an odd number, and
    • \(C\) the event of rolling at least a 2.
  • Then:
    1. \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive
    2. \(A\) and \(B\) are collectively exhaustive
    3. \(B\) and \(C\) are collectively exhaustive
    4. Are \(B\) and \(C\) mutually exclusive?

The unconditional from joint probability rule

  • A set of events that is mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive is called a partition.

  • The unconditional from joint probability rule says \[p(A)=\sum_{\text{all } B} p(A\cap B),\] where the sum is taken over events \(B\) that form a partition.

    to obtain an unconditional probability from joint probabilities, we sum the joint probabilities over all possible events in a partition

Unconditional from joint rule

  • Suppose you had the set of joint probabilities, say, \(p(\text{face card}\cap\text{heart})\), \(p(\text{face card}\cap\text{club})\), \(p(\text{face card}\cap\text{spade})\), and \(p(\text{face card}\cap\text{diamond})\)
  • You need to compute the unconditional probability of obtaining a face card
  • Using the unconditional from joint probability rule:

\[\begin{aligned} p(\text{face card})&=\sum_{\text{all suits}} p(\text{face card}\cap\text{suit})\\ &= p(\text{face card}\cap\text{heart}) + p(\text{face card}\cap\text{club}) \\ &\quad + p(\text{face card}\cap\text{spade}) + p(\text{face card}\cap\text{diamond})\\ &=\frac{3}{52}+\frac{3}{52}+\frac{3}{52}+\frac{3}{52}=\frac{12}{52}. \end{aligned}\]

  • This gives the correct answer, because we already knew that \(p(\text{face card}) = 12/52.\)

Part III: Joint Probability Tables

Joint probability tables

  • A joint probability table shows probabilities for joint events.
  • Generic layout (rows = \(A\) events, columns = \(B\) events):
\(B_1\) \(B_2\) Total
\(A_1\) \(P(A_1 \cap B_1)\) \(P(A_1 \cap B_2)\) \(P(A_1)\)
\(A_2\) \(P(A_2 \cap B_1)\) \(P(A_2 \cap B_2)\) \(P(A_2)\)
Total \(P(B_1)\) \(P(B_2)\) \(1\)

  • Census of first-year engineering students:
Passed Failed # Students
Male 3,800 4,700 8,500
Female 1,600 2,400 4,000
# Students 5,400 7,100 Total = 12,500

Passed Failed Marginal Probability
Male 0.304 0.376 0.680
Female 0.128 0.192 0.320
Marginal Probability 0.432 0.568 1.000
  • We will use this table to read entries, compute marginals, and compute conditionals.
  • We read entries in this table as \(P(\text{male }\cap\text{ failed}) = 0.376.\)
  • Numbers in the bottom row and right column are unconditional probabilities:
    • \(P(\text{passed}) = 0.432\) represents the proportion of students who passed (regardless of their sex).

Engineering students: conditional rates

  • Goal: compare failure rates for males and females.
  • Notation: \(p(\text{failed}|\text{male})\) and \(p(\text{failed}|\text{female})\).
  • Male failure rate (table): \[p(\text{failed}|\text{male}) = \frac{0.376}{0.680} = 0.5529...\]
  • Female failure rate (table): \[p(\text{failed}|\text{female}) = \frac{0.192}{0.320} = 0.600.\]
  • Check against raw counts: \(4{,}700/8{,}500\) and \(2{,}400/4{,}000\).

Labor market: counts to probabilities

  • Suppose a survey of 1,000 adults records employment status and education.
  • Start with counts:
Employed Unemployed # Adults
College 360 40 400
No college 540 60 600
# Adults 900 100 1000
  • Divide by 1,000 to convert counts to joint probabilities.
    • \(P(\text{college} \cap \text{unemployed}) = 40/1000 = 0.04\)
    • \(P(\text{college}) = 400/1000 = 0.40\)
    • \(P(\text{unemployed} \mid \text{college})=\frac{0.04}{0.40}=0.10.\)

Part IV: Multiplication Laws

Multiplication laws

  • The general multiplication law is \[p(A\cap B)=p(A)\times p(B|A)=p(B)\times p(A|B).\]

    The joint probability of two events equals the unconditional probability of one event times the conditional probability of the other event, given that the first event has already occurred.

  • This is not a new rule at all! We have previously defined \[p(A|B) = p(A \cap B) / p(B),\] and this rule just rearranges that.

Unemployment by education

  • Let \(A\) be “adult is unemployed” and \(B\) be “adult has a college degree”
  • If \(P(B)=0.40\) and \(P(A \mid B)=0.05\), then \[P(A \cap B)=P(B)\,P(A\mid B)=0.40\times0.05=0.02.\]
    • Interpretation: 2% of adults are both unemployed and college‑educated.
  • Also:
    • \(P(A \mid B^c)=\frac{P(A\cap B^c)}{P(B^c)}\)
    • if \(P(A \mid B^c)=0.10\), then \(P(A \cap B^c)=P(B^c)\,P(A\mid B^c)=0.60\times0.10=0.06.\)
      • Interpretation: 6% of adults are unemployed and do not have a college degree.
    • Combine the two groups:
      • \(P(A)=P(A\cap B)+P(A\cap B^c)=0.02+0.06=0.08.\)
      • Interpretation: overall unemployment is 8%.

Independence and dependence

  • Events \(A\) and \(B\) are dependent when the probability of \(A\) is affected by occurrence of \(B\), \[P(A) \ne P(A|B).\]
  • Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if and only if \(p(A)= p(A|B)\).
    • if learning that \(B\) happened does not change the probability of \(A\), then \(A\) and \(B\) are independent

College and unemployment

  • Let \(A\) be “has a college degree” and \(B\) be “is unemployed”.
  • Suppose \(P(B)=0.08\) but \(P(B\mid A)=0.05\).
  • Then \(P(B\mid A)\ne P(B)\), so \(A\) and \(B\) are dependent.
  • Interpretation: unemployment is lower among college graduates in this example.

Two households, same city

  • Let \(A\) be “a randomly selected household rents” and \(B\) be “a second, independently selected household rents.”
  • Suppose \(P(A)=0.35\) and \(P(B)=0.35\), and sampling is independent.
  • Then \(P(A\mid B)=0.35\), so \(P(A\mid B)=P(A)\).
  • Interpretation: knowing one household rents does not change the probability for another, independently selected household.

Union status and wages

  • Let \(A\) be “worker is unionized” and \(B\) be “worker earns above the median wage”.
  • Suppose \(P(B)=0.50\) but \(P(B\mid A)=0.65\).
  • Then \(P(B\mid A)\ne P(B)\), so \(A\) and \(B\) are dependent.
  • Interpretation: union status is associated with higher wages in this example.

Independent lab tests

  • A diagnostic test has sensitivity 0.92:
    • \(P(\text{positive}\mid\text{disease})=0.92\)
  • Two independent labs test the same patient who has the disease.
  • Then \[P(\text{both positive}\mid\text{disease})=0.92\times0.92=0.8464.\]
  • Interpretation: with independence, the joint conditional probability is the product of the conditional probabilities.

Exercise: Ride-share

Let \(A\) be “a ride‑share driver logs into the app” and let \(B\) be “surge pricing is active.” Suppose \(P(A)=0.40\), \(P(B)=0.30\), and \(P(A\cap B)=0.18\).

  • Question (1 min)
  • Answers
  • Are \(A\) and \(B\) independent?
  • \(P(A)P(B)=0.40\times0.30=0.12\)
  • Since \(P(A\cap B)=0.18\ne0.12\), the events are not independent.
  • \(P(A\mid B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)=0.18/0.30=0.60\), which is higher than \(P(A)=0.40\).
  • Interpretation: on surge days, more drivers are likely to log in, and on non‑surge days fewer log in, so we expect \(P(A\mid B)\) to be higher than \(P(A)\).

Exercise: Repeat purchase in consecutive months

Let \(A\) be “a customer makes a purchase in January” and \(B\) be “the same customer makes a purchase in February.” Suppose \(P(A)=0.25\), \(P(B)=0.30\), and \(P(A\cap B)=0.10\).

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Are \(A\) and \(B\) independent? Compute \(P(A\mid B)\).
  • \(P(A)P(B)=0.25\times0.30=0.075\), so \(P(A\cap B)\ne P(A)P(B)\).
  • Not independent.
  • \(P(A\mid B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)=0.10/0.30=0.333\overline{3}\).

Special multiplication law

  • The special multiplication law says that the joint probability of \(A\) and \(B\) is the product of the unconditional, or marginal, probabilities of \(A\) and \(B\).
  • Formally: if \(A\) and \(B\) are independent, then \[p(A\cap B)=p(A)\times p(B).\]
    • if events do not affect each other, multiply their marginal probabilities

Exercise: A fair die

A single fair die is rolled. Let \(A=\{3\}\) and \(B=\{1,3,5\}\).

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers

Are \(A\) and \(B\) independent?

  1. No! If it is given that \(A\) occurred then we know that \(B\) occurred!
  2. Compute all three probabilities \(P(A)=1/6\), \(P(B)=1/2\), and \(P(A \cap B)=P(\{3\})=1/6\). Since the product \(P(A) \times P(B)=(1/6)(1/2)=1/12\) is not the same number as \(P(A\cap B)=1/6\), the events A and B are not independent.

Exercise: Two coin flips

You flip a coin twice. Let \(A_1\) be the event that you obtain a H on the first throw, and let \(A_2\) be the event that you obtain a H on the second throw. Assume that \(A_1\) and \(A_2\) are independent.

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  1. Is it reasonable to assume that \(A_1\) and \(A_2\) are independent?
  2. What is the probability of the event \(B\), that you throw heads on both throws?
  1. Yes. We believe that the outcome of one coin flip does not affect the outcome of the next one. (But: “Gambler’s fallacy”.)
  2. Because the events are independent, we can use the special multiplication rule \[P(B) = P(A_1 \cap A_2) = P(A_1)P(A_2) = 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25.\]

Exercise: Chess game

You are going to play a game of chess. We will flip a coin. The event \(A\) is whether it comes up H and you will play with white. If not (T), you will play with black. Let the event \(B\) correspond to winning the game. If you play with white, you win 70% of the time. If you play with black, you win 50% of the time. Assume \(P(A) = 0.5\) (“fair coin”).

  • Question (homework)
  • Answers
  1. What is \(P(B|A)\)?
  2. What is the probability that the coin comes up H and that you win the game?
  3. What is the probability that you win the game?
  4. You win the game. What is the probability that the coin came up H?
  5. Are \(A\) and \(B\) independent?
  6. Cheating is common in chess. One way to cheat is to introduce an unfair coin. If \(P(A)=0.9\), what is \(P(B)\)?
  • \(P(B\mid A)=0.70\)
  • \(P(A\cap B)=0.5\times0.70=0.35\)
  • \(P(B)=0.5\times0.70+0.5\times0.50=0.60\)
  • \(P(A\mid B)=0.35/0.60\approx0.583\)
  • Not independent, since \(P(B\mid A)=0.70\ne P(B)=0.60\)
  • If \(P(A)=0.9\), then \(P(B)=0.9\times0.70+0.1\times0.50=0.68\)

Exercise: Health coverage and health status

A telephone survey classifies adults by health insurance and self-reported health. The joint probability table is:

Excellent Not excellent Total
Insured 0.21 0.66 0.87
Uninsured 0.02 0.11 0.13
Total 0.23 0.77 1.00
  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  1. What is the probability that a randomly chosen individual reports excellent health given that they are insured?
  2. What is the same probability given that they are uninsured?
  3. Are health coverage and excellent health independent?
  1. \(P(\text{excellent} \mid \text{insured}) = 0.21 / 0.87 \approx 0.241\)
  2. \(P(\text{excellent} \mid \text{uninsured}) = 0.02 / 0.13 \approx 0.154\)
  3. \(P(\text{excellent}) = 0.23\) but \(P(\text{excellent} \mid \text{insured}) \approx 0.241 \ne 0.23\), so the events are not independent. Insured individuals are more likely to report excellent health in this sample.
