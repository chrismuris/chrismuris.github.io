  • But the shape of the distribution changes too
  • For large \(n\), does this distribution look familiar?
  • How about like this?

Sampling distribution and the CLT

  • The sampling distribution of a statistic is the probability distribution of that statistic
  • This is the distribution we use to compute probabilities involving sample statistics
  • The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says:
    • for random samples of size \(n\geq 30\), the sample mean is approximately normal
    • if \(n\) is large, then \(\bar X\) is approximately normal with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\)
  • The CLT lets us make probability statements about the sample mean relative to the population mean
  • The original distribution can be any distribution, as long as \(n\) is large enough:
    • voter polling
    • dice throws
    • student GPA

Weekly food expenditures (CLT example)

  • Weekly food expenditures follow some distribution with mean \(\mu=120\) and standard deviation \(\sigma=12\)
  • Take a random sample of \(n=100\) students and compute \(\bar X\)
  • By the CLT, \(\bar X\) is approximately normal with mean 120 and sd \(12/\sqrt{100}=1.2\)
  • Standardize: \[P(119\le \bar X \le 121) \approx P(-0.83 \le Z \le 0.83)\]
  • Using a normal table or pnorm: \(0.7967-0.2033=0.5934\)
  • About a 59% chance that \(\bar X\) is within $1 of \(\mu\)
  • If \(n=10{,}000\), \(\sigma_{\bar X} = 12/\sqrt{10000} = 0.12\), so \(\bar X\) concentrates tightly around \(\mu\)
  • If \(\sigma = 120\), \(\sigma_{\bar X} = 120/\sqrt{100} = 12\), so more population variability means less precision

Household credit card debt

  • Household credit card debt follows a right-skewed distribution with \(\mu = 15{,}250\) and \(\sigma = 7{,}125\)
  • A consumer group surveys \(n = 200\) households
  • By the CLT, \(\bar X\) is approximately normal with mean \(15{,}250\) and sd \(7{,}125/\sqrt{200} \approx 503.8\)
  • \(P(\bar X > 16{,}000) = P\left(Z > \frac{16000 - 15250}{503.8}\right) = P(Z > 1.49) \approx 0.068\)
1 - pnorm(16000, mean = 15250, sd = 7125 / sqrt(200))
[1] 0.06829032

Part II: The Sample Proportion

Sample proportion basics

  • The sample proportion is a special type of sample mean, with all measurements 0 or 1
  • It shows up in polling, defect rates, and any yes/no outcome
  • To understand the sample proportion, we use the binomial distribution
  • Goal: use the CLT to approximate probabilities about \(\widehat P\) when \(n\) is large

Binomial random variables in practice

  • Number of boys in a family of 3: \(\text{Binom}(n=3, p=0.5)\)
  • Number of heads in 20 flips: \(\text{Binom}(n=20, p=0.5)\)
  • Number of female students in a sample of 125 from a population that is 57% female: \(\text{Binom}(n=125, p=0.57)\)
  • Correct answers on a 15-question multiple choice test (4 options), guessing: \(\text{Binom}(n=15, p=0.25)\)
  • Example 3 holds the key to understanding Part C of this course

Estimating the Trudeau vote share with a poll of 100

  • You are a pollster estimating the proportion of Canadian voters who will vote for Trudeau
  • The population proportion is unknown: say it’s \(p = 0.55\)
  • You select \(n = 100\) voters via simple random sampling and count the Trudeau voters (\(X\))
  • Then \(X \sim \text{Binom}(100, 0.55)\)
    • mean of \(X\): \(\mu = np = 55\)
    • standard deviation of \(X\): \(\sigma = \sqrt{npq} = \sqrt{100 \times 0.55 \times 0.45} \approx 4.97\)

From counts to proportions

  • Let \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) be 0/1 indicators (1 success, 0 failure)
  • Then \(X=\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\) and the sample proportion is \[\widehat P = X/n = \bar X\]
  • So \(\widehat P\) is a sample mean of 0/1 data
  • If the population proportion is \(p\), then
    • \(\mu_{\widehat P} = p\)
    • \(\sigma_{\widehat P} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\)
  • Translation table:
    • population mean \(\mu\) ↔︎ proportion \(p\)
    • sample mean \(\bar X\) ↔︎ sample proportion \(\widehat P\)
    • population variance \(\sigma^2\) ↔︎ \(p(1-p)\)
  • Worked example: \(p=0.30\), \(n=100\)
    • \(\mu_{\widehat P} = 0.30\)
    • \(\sigma_{\widehat P} = \sqrt{0.30 \times 0.70 / 100} \approx 0.0458\)

The CLT condition for proportions

  • Because the sample proportion \(\widehat P\) is a special case of a sample mean, the CLT applies
  • But we need to be a bit more careful about the large enough sample size:
    • Shafer and Zhang wants you to check whether a certain interval lies wholly in \([0,1]\)
    • We will take a shortcut, see next slide
  • The CLT for sample proportions says that if \(np\ge 5\) and \(n(1-p)\ge 5\), then \(\widehat P\) is approximately normal
    • mean: \(\mu_{\widehat P} = p\)
    • standard deviation: \(\sigma_{\widehat P} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\)

Polling example: binomial vs normal approximation

  • Let \(X\) be the number in the sample who vote for Trudeau: \(X \sim \text{Bin}(100,0.55)\)
  • The question asks \[P(\widehat P > 0.5) = P(X > 50) = 1-P(X \leq 49)\]
  • Tables do not go to \(n=100\), but you can compute the exact binomial probability in R:
1 - pbinom(49, 100, 0.55)
[1] 0.8654238
  • Because \(np = 55 \geq 5\) and \(n(1-p)=45\geq 5\), the CLT says that \(\widehat P\) is approximately normal with mean 0.55 and standard deviation:
sqrt(0.55 * (1 - 0.55) / 100)
[1] 0.04974937
  • The normal approximation is \[P(\widehat P > 0.5) = P\left(Z > \frac{0.5-0.55}{0.04974937}\right)\]
1 - pnorm((0.5 - 0.55) / sqrt(0.55 * (1 - 0.55) / 100))
[1] 0.8425607
  • Compare the two results, then repeat the normal approximation with \(n=1000\)
1 - pnorm((0.5 - 0.55) / sqrt(0.55 * (1 - 0.55) / 1000))
[1] 0.9992591

Exercise: CLT condition for \(\widehat P\)

A population has \(p=0.08\) and a sample size \(n=50\).

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Check whether the CLT for proportions applies
  • \(np=50 \times 0.08=4\) and \(n(1-p)=50 \times 0.92=46\)
  • Since \(np<5\), the CLT condition is not met

Exercise: Approximate a proportion

A poll has \(n=400\) and observed \(\widehat P=0.62\). Assume the true \(p=0.60\).

  • Question (2 min)
  • Answers
  • Approximate \(P(\widehat P \ge 0.62)\) using the CLT for proportions
  • \(\sigma_{\widehat P}=\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}=\sqrt{0.60 \times 0.40 / 400}=0.0245\)
  • Standardize: \(z=(0.62-0.60)/0.0245 \approx 0.82\)
  • \(P(\widehat P \ge 0.62)\approx P(Z \ge 0.82)=1-0.7939=0.2061\)

Exercise: Pollster CLT

In the pollster example, \(p=0.55\) and \(n=100\).

  • Question (3 min)

Exercise: Cholesterol and the CLT (SZ 6.2, 15)

The mean cholesterol in large eggs is 186 mg with sd 7 mg. A sample of 144 eggs is taken.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Find \(P(184 < \bar X < 188)\)
  • By the CLT, \(\bar X\) is approximately normal with mean 186 and sd \(7/\sqrt{144} = 7/12 \approx 0.583\)
  • Standardize: \(z_1 = (184 - 186)/(7/12) = -24/7 \approx -3.43\) and \(z_2 = (188 - 186)/(7/12) = 24/7 \approx 3.43\)
  • \(P(184 < \bar X < 188) = P(-3.43 < Z < 3.43) \approx 0.9994\)
pnorm(188, mean = 186, sd = 7 / sqrt(144)) -
  pnorm(184, mean = 186, sd = 7 / sqrt(144))
[1] 0.9993932

Exercise: Defective light bulbs

A factory produces light bulbs with a 4% defect rate (\(p = 0.04\)). A shipment contains \(n = 400\) bulbs.

  • Question (3 min)
  • Answers
  • Verify that the CLT for proportions applies
  • Using the normal approximation, find the probability that the sample defect rate exceeds 5%
  • Check: \(np = 400 \times 0.04 = 16 \geq 5\) and \(n(1-p) = 400 \times 0.96 = 384 \geq 5\), so the CLT applies
  • \(\sigma_{\widehat P} = \sqrt{0.04 \times 0.96 / 400} = \sqrt{0.000096} \approx 0.0098\)
  • \(P(\widehat P > 0.05) = P\left(Z > \frac{0.05 - 0.04}{0.0098}\right) = P(Z > 1.02) \approx 0.154\)
sd_p <- sqrt(0.04 * 0.96 / 400)
1 - pnorm((0.05 - 0.04) / sd_p)
[1] 0.1537171
